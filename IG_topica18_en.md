## 📘 Topic 18: Variation and Selection — Detailed Notes

---

### 18.1 Variation (变异)

#### 🔹 Definition

- **Variation**: the **differences between individuals of the same species**.
- Variation exists in all populations — no two individuals (except identical twins) are exactly alike.

#### 🔹 Types of Variation

| Feature | Continuous Variation | Discontinuous Variation |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Definition** | Results in a **range of phenotypes** between two extremes | Results in a **limited number of phenotypes** with **no intermediates** |
| **Graph shape** | Bell-shaped curve (normal distribution) | Bar chart with distinct categories |
| **Examples** | Body length, body mass, height, weight, skin colour | ABO blood groups, seed shape in peas, seed colour in peas, gender (male/female) |
| **Causes** | Caused by **both genes and the environment** | Usually caused by **genes only** |

**Continuous Variation Example:**
- Human height: individuals range from very short to very tall, with most people somewhere in between.
- The environment also plays a role (e.g. nutrition affects height).

**Discontinuous Variation Example:**
- ABO blood groups: you can only be A, B, AB, or O — there is no "in-between" blood type.
- Pea seed shape: either round or wrinkled — no intermediate shapes.

#### 🔹 Mutation (突变)

- **Mutation**: a **genetic change** (a change in the DNA).
- Mutation is the **way in which new alleles are formed** — it is the **ultimate source of all genetic variation**.
- **Factors that increase mutation rate**:
  - **Ionising radiation** (e.g. X-rays, UV radiation, gamma rays)
  - **Some chemicals** (e.g. certain toxins, carcinogens)

#### 🔹 Supplement: Gene Mutation

- **Gene mutation**: a **random change in the base sequence of DNA**.
  - This can involve substitution, insertion, or deletion of bases.
  - A change in base sequence may lead to a different amino acid being coded for, potentially altering the protein produced.

#### 🔹 Sources of Genetic Variation (Supplement)

There are **four main sources** of genetic variation in populations:

| Source | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mutation** | Random changes in DNA base sequences create **new alleles** |
| **Meiosis** | During meiosis, chromosomes are shuffled, producing genetically different gametes |
| **Random mating** | Individuals mate randomly, mixing alleles in the population |
| **Random fertilisation** | Which sperm fertilises which egg is random, creating unique combinations of alleles |

### 18.2 Adaptive Features (适应性特征)

#### 🔹 Definition

- **Adaptive feature**: an **inherited feature** that helps an organism to **survive and reproduce** in its environment.
- These features are the result of evolution by natural selection over many generations.

#### 🔹 Core Requirement

- You should be able to **interpret images or other information** about a species to **describe its adaptive features**.
  - Example: A polar bear has thick fur and a layer of blubber — adaptive features for surviving in cold environments.
  - Example: A camel has humps storing fat, long eyelashes, and the ability to go without water for long periods — adaptive features for desert life.

#### 🔹 Supplement: Hydrophytes & Xerophytes

##### 🌊 Hydrophytes (水生植物) — Plants adapted to living in water

| Adaptive Feature | Explanation |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Thin or no cuticle** | Cuticle prevents water loss, but hydrophytes don't need to conserve water; a thin cuticle allows gas exchange and water absorption directly through the surface |
| **Stomata on upper surface** | In floating plants (e.g. water lily), stomata are on the **upper surface** of leaves (not the lower surface) to access air |
| **Air spaces (aerenchyma)** | Large air spaces in stems and leaves provide **buoyancy** (floatation) and allow oxygen to diffuse to submerged parts |
| **Reduced roots** | Water and minerals can be absorbed directly through stems and leaves, so roots are often small or absent |
| **Flexible stems** | Stems are flexible to withstand water currents without breaking |

##### 🏜️ Xerophytes (旱生植物) — Plants adapted to living in dry/arid conditions

| Adaptive Feature | Explanation |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Thick cuticle** | A waxy, thick cuticle reduces water loss from the surface of leaves and stems |
| **Sunken stomata** | Stomata are located in pits (depressions) to trap moist air, reducing the concentration gradient and slowing water loss |
| **Reduced leaf surface area** | Leaves may be small (e.g. pine needles) or absent (e.g. cactus spines) to reduce the surface area for transpiration |
| **Water storage tissue** | Succulent stems or leaves store water for use during dry periods (e.g. cactus) |
| **Hairy leaves** | Hairs trap a layer of moist air near the leaf surface, reducing the water concentration gradient |
| **Rolled leaves** | Some xerophytes (e.g. marram grass) can roll their leaves to hide stomata from the wind |
| **Deep root systems** | Extensive roots reach deep underground water sources or spread widely to absorb any available moisture |

### 18.3 Selection (选择)

#### 🔹 Natural Selection (自然选择) — Core

Natural selection is the process by which organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully. The key steps are:

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **(a) Genetic variation** | Individuals within a population show **genetic variation** (due to mutation, meiosis, random fertilisation) |
| **(b) Overproduction** | Populations produce **many offspring** — more than the environment can support |
| **(c) Struggle for survival** | There is a **struggle for survival** due to limited resources (food, water, shelter, mates) — this includes **competition** |
| **(d) Differential survival & reproduction** | Individuals with **adaptive features** that are better suited to the environment are **more likely to survive and reproduce** |
| **(e) Inheritance of advantageous alleles** | These surviving individuals **pass on their advantageous alleles** to the next generation |

> Over many generations, the **frequency of advantageous alleles increases** in the population, and the population becomes **better adapted** to its environment.

#### 🔹 Supplement: Adaptation as a Process

- **Adaptation** (as a process): the process, **resulting from natural selection**, by which populations become **more suited to their environment over many generations**.
- It is not individuals that adapt — it is **populations** that change over time.

#### 🔹 Supplement: Antibiotic Resistance in Bacteria

This is a classic example of natural selection in action:

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Within a bacterial population, there is **genetic variation** — some bacteria are naturally **resistant** to an antibiotic (due to a mutation) |
| 2 | The antibiotic is applied, killing **non-resistant** bacteria |
| 3 | **Resistant bacteria survive** and reproduce |
| 4 | They **pass on the resistance allele** to their offspring |
| 5 | Over time, the population becomes **mostly or entirely resistant** to the antibiotic |
| 6 | The antibiotic becomes **ineffective** against that strain of bacteria |

> ⚠️ This is why **overuse of antibiotics** is dangerous — it accelerates the development of antibiotic-resistant "superbugs" like MRSA.

#### 🔹 Selective Breeding (人工选择 / 选择性育种) — Core

- **Selective breeding (artificial selection)**: **humans** select individuals with **desirable features** and breed them together.

**Process:**

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Select** individuals with desirable features from a population |
| 2 | **Cross (breed)** these selected individuals to produce the next generation |
| 3 | From the offspring, **select** those that show the desirable features most strongly |
| 4 | Repeat this process **over many generations** |

**Examples of selective breeding:**

| Area | Examples of Desirable Features |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **Crop plants** | Larger fruits, higher yield, disease resistance, drought tolerance, better taste |
| **Domesticated animals** | Higher milk production (cows), more meat (chickens, pigs), faster growth, docile temperament (dogs) |

#### 🔹 Differences Between Natural and Artificial Selection (Supplement)

| Feature | Natural Selection | Artificial (Selective) Selection |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Who selects?** | The **environment** (nature) | **Humans** |
| **Selection pressure** | Predators, climate, food availability, disease | Human preference for certain traits |
| **Survival criteria** | Best adapted to the environment | Possession of desirable features (from a human perspective) |
| **Speed** | Usually slow (many generations) | Can be relatively fast (humans actively breed selected individuals) |
| **Outcome** | Populations become better adapted to their natural environment | Organisms may have traits useful to humans but not necessarily beneficial in the wild |
