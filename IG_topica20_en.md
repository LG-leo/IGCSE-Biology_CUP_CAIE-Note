## 📘 Topic 20: Human Influences on Ecosystems — Detailed Notes

---

### 20.1 Food Supply (食物供应)

#### 🔹 How Humans Have Increased Food Production

| Method | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Agricultural machinery** | Machines (tractors, harvesters) allow farming of **larger areas of land** and **improve efficiency** (faster planting, harvesting, processing) |
| **(b) Chemical fertilisers** | Provide essential nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium) to crops, **improving yields** |
| **(c) Insecticides** | Kill insects that damage crops, **improving quality and yield** |
| **(d) Herbicides** | Kill weeds, **reducing competition** with crop plants for light, water, and nutrients |
| **(e) Selective breeding** | Breeding organisms with desirable traits to **improve production** by crop plants and livestock (e.g. higher yield, disease resistance, faster growth) |

#### 🔹 Monocultures (单一作物种植) — Advantages & Disadvantages

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|---------------|
| Efficient to plant, manage, and harvest large areas | **Reduces biodiversity** — only one species/variety is grown |
| Higher yields per unit area | **More vulnerable to disease** — if one plant gets infected, the entire crop can be wiped out |
| Easier to use machinery | **Soil exhaustion** — growing the same crop repeatedly depletes specific nutrients from the soil |
| | **Increased need for fertilisers and pesticides** — monocultures attract more pests and require more chemical inputs |

#### 🔹 Intensive Livestock Production (集约化畜牧业) — Advantages & Disadvantages

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Higher productivity** — animals grow faster and produce more meat/milk/eggs | **Animal welfare concerns** — animals may be kept in crowded, unnatural conditions |
| **Lower cost** — more efficient production reduces food prices | **Increased disease risk** — crowded conditions allow diseases to spread rapidly |
| **Year-round production** — controlled environments allow continuous production | **High energy use** — heating, lighting, ventilation require large amounts of energy |
| **Less land required** — animals are kept in confined spaces | **Waste disposal problems** — large amounts of animal waste can pollute water sources |

### 20.2 Habitat Destruction (栖息地破坏)

#### 🔹 Biodiversity (生物多样性)

- **Biodiversity**: the **number of different species** that live in an area.
- High biodiversity = a healthy, stable ecosystem.

#### 🔹 Reasons for Habitat Destruction

| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Increased area for housing** | Growing human population needs more land for housing and urban development |
| **(b) Increased area for crop plant production** | More land is cleared for farming to produce food |
| **(c) Increased area for livestock production** | Land is cleared for grazing animals or growing animal feed |
| **(d) Extraction of natural resources** | Mining, quarrying, logging, and oil drilling destroy natural habitats |
| **(e) Freshwater and marine pollution** | Pollution from industry, agriculture, and sewage damages aquatic habitats |

#### 🔹 Human Impact Through Food Webs

- By **altering food webs and food chains**, humans can have a **negative impact on habitats**.
- Examples: overfishing removes a key species, causing ripple effects through the food web; introducing a new predator can wipe out native species.

#### 🔹 Deforestation (森林砍伐) — Undesirable Effects

| Effect | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Reducing biodiversity** | Forests are home to millions of species; destroying them causes loss of habitats and species |
| **Extinction** | Many species cannot survive habitat loss and become extinct |
| **Loss of soil** | Tree roots hold soil in place; without trees, soil is eroded by wind and rain |
| **Flooding** | Without trees to absorb water, rainwater runs off quickly, causing floods |
| **Increase of CO₂ in the atmosphere** | Trees absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis; deforestation releases stored carbon and reduces CO₂ absorption, contributing to climate change |

### 20.3 Pollution (污染)

#### 🔹 Water Pollution — Untreated Sewage & Excess Fertiliser

| Pollutant | Effects on Aquatic Ecosystems |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Untreated sewage** | Contains organic matter and pathogens → decomposers break it down using oxygen → **oxygen levels drop** → fish and other aquatic organisms die |
| **Excess fertiliser** | Runs off from farmland into rivers and lakes → causes **eutrophication** (see below) |

#### 🔹 Eutrophication (富营养化) — Supplement

Eutrophication is the process by which excess nutrients (nitrates, phosphates) cause severe damage to aquatic ecosystems. The steps are:

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Increased availability of nitrate and other ions** — fertilisers (or sewage) wash into rivers/lakes |
| 2 | **Increased growth of producers** — algae and aquatic plants grow rapidly (algal bloom) |
| 3 | **Increased decomposition after death** — when the algae die, decomposers (bacteria) break them down |
| 4 | **Increased aerobic respiration by decomposers** — decomposers use up large amounts of dissolved oxygen |
| 5 | **Reduction in dissolved oxygen** — oxygen levels in the water drop dramatically |
| 6 | **Death of organisms requiring dissolved oxygen** — fish and other aquatic animals suffocate and die |

> **Summary**: Excess nutrients → algal bloom → algae die → decomposers multiply → oxygen depleted → fish die

#### 🔹 Non-biodegradable Plastics

- **Non-biodegradable plastics** do not break down naturally in the environment.
- **Effects in aquatic ecosystems**: Animals ingest plastic (mistaking it for food) → blocks digestive system → starvation and death; microplastics enter the food chain.
- **Effects in terrestrial ecosystems**: Plastics accumulate in soil, preventing water absorption; animals can become entangled in plastic waste.

#### 🔹 Air Pollution — Methane & Carbon Dioxide

| Gas | Source | Effect |
|-----|--------|--------|
| **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** | Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), deforestation, industrial processes | **Enhanced greenhouse effect** → global warming → **climate change** |
| **Methane (CH₄)** | Cattle farming (digestive processes), rice paddies, landfill sites, natural gas leaks | **Enhanced greenhouse effect** (methane is a more potent greenhouse gas than CO₂) |

**The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect & Climate Change:**

- Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄) trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere.
- Human activities have **increased the concentration** of these gases → **enhanced greenhouse effect** → global temperatures rise → **climate change** (extreme weather, rising sea levels, habitat loss).

### 20.4 Conservation (保护)

#### 🔹 Sustainable Resource (可持续资源)

- **Sustainable resource**: a resource that is **produced as rapidly as it is removed from the environment** so that it does not run out.
- Examples: sustainable forestry (replanting trees as fast as they are cut), sustainable fishing (catching fish at a rate that allows populations to recover).

#### 🔹 Resources That Can Be Conserved Sustainably

| Resource | Conservation Methods |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Forests** | Education, protected areas, quotas, replanting |
| **Fish stocks** | Education, closed seasons, protected areas, controlled net types and mesh size, quotas, monitoring |

#### 🔹 Why Organisms Become Endangered or Extinct

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Climate change** | Changing temperatures and weather patterns make habitats unsuitable |
| **Habitat destruction** | Forests, wetlands, and other habitats are cleared for human use |
| **Hunting** | Animals are killed for food, fur, ivory, or sport |
| **Overharvesting** | Plants and animals are removed faster than they can reproduce |
| **Pollution** | Toxic chemicals, plastics, and waste damage habitats and harm organisms |
| **Introduced species** | Non-native species outcompete, prey on, or bring diseases to native species |

#### 🔹 How Endangered Species Can Be Conserved

| Method | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Monitoring and protecting species and habitats** | Scientists track populations and protect key habitats (e.g. national parks, nature reserves) |
| **(b) Education** | Teaching people about the importance of biodiversity and how to protect it |
| **(c) Captive breeding programmes** | Breeding endangered species in zoos or specialised facilities, then reintroducing them into the wild |
| **(d) Seed banks** | Storing seeds of endangered plant species to preserve genetic diversity and allow future replanting |

#### 🔹 Forest Conservation Methods (Supplement)

| Method | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Education** | Teaching sustainable forestry practices and the importance of forests |
| **Protected areas** | Establishing national parks and reserves where logging is prohibited |
| **Quotas** | Limiting the amount of timber that can be harvested |
| **Replanting** | Planting new trees to replace those that have been cut down |

#### 🔹 Fish Stock Conservation Methods (Supplement)

| Method | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Education** | Teaching fishermen about sustainable fishing practices |
| **Closed seasons** | Banning fishing during breeding seasons to allow fish populations to recover |
| **Protected areas** | Creating marine reserves where fishing is not allowed |
| **Controlled net types and mesh size** | Using nets that allow young/juvenile fish to escape (larger mesh size) |
| **Quotas** | Limiting the number of fish that can be caught |
| **Monitoring** | Tracking fish populations to ensure they are not being overfished |

#### 🔹 Reasons for Conservation Programmes

| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **(a) Maintaining or increasing biodiversity** | High biodiversity makes ecosystems more stable and resilient |
| **(b) Reducing extinction** | Protecting species from disappearing forever |
| **(c) Protecting vulnerable ecosystems** | Safeguarding habitats that are easily damaged (e.g. rainforests, coral reefs) |
| **(d) Maintaining ecosystem functions** | Including: |
| | • **Nutrient cycling** — decomposers recycle nutrients, keeping soil fertile |
| | • **Resource provision** — ecosystems provide food, drugs (medicines), fuel (wood), and genes (genetic material for breeding) |

#### 🔹 Use of AI and IVF in Captive Breeding (Supplement)

| Technique | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Artificial insemination (AI)** | Sperm is collected from a male and artificially inserted into a female, allowing breeding without the animals needing to meet (useful for endangered species in different locations) |
| **In vitro fertilisation (IVF)** | Eggs are fertilised by sperm outside the body (in a lab), then the embryos are implanted into a surrogate female — increases the number of offspring produced |

#### 🔹 Risks of Reduced Population Size (Supplement)

- When a species' **population size decreases**, its **genetic variation** also decreases.
- **Risks include**:
  - **Inbreeding** — closely related individuals breed, increasing the chance of harmful recessive alleles being expressed
  - **Reduced ability to adapt** — with less genetic variation, the population is less able to adapt to environmental changes (e.g. new diseases, climate change)
  - **Increased extinction risk** — a small, genetically uniform population is much more vulnerable to extinction

> ⚠️ Knowledge of genetic drift is **not required**.
