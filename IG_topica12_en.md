## 1. The Nature of Respiration

### 1.1 What is respiration?
- **Respiration** is a series of **enzyme-catalysed** chemical reactions that take place in all living cells. Its central task is to release the energy stored in the chemical bonds of organic nutrients such as **glucose** and transfer it to **ATP (adenosine triphosphate)** molecules.
- ATP is the only form of energy that cells can use directly, often called the cell’s “energy currency”. The energy released when ATP is hydrolysed can immediately drive a wide range of energy‑requiring activities inside the cell.
- It is essential to stress that respiration is **not the same as breathing**. These terms must never be used interchangeably in an exam. **Respiration** is a chemical process inside cells, whereas **breathing** (or ventilation) is a physical process involving the movement of the ribcage and the flow of air into and out of the lungs. Confusing these two concepts is one of the most common causes of lost marks.

### 1.2 The universality of respiration
- Respiration occurs **continuously** in **all living cells**. Whether plant cells, animal cells, fungi or bacteria, every cell must carry out some form of respiration in order to stay alive.
- During the day, plant cells carry out both **photosynthesis** and **respiration**. Photosynthesis occurs only in cells that contain chloroplasts, whereas respiration occurs in every living cell of the plant.
- At night, photosynthesis stops but respiration continues. At this time the plant as a whole takes in oxygen and gives out carbon dioxide.

## 2. What is the energy released by respiration used for?

The ATP produced by respiration is used in the following seven essential energy‑consuming processes. These processes are all required for building, maintaining and reproducing life.

### 2.1 Muscle contraction
- ATP provides energy for the actin and myosin filaments in muscle cells to slide past each other, shortening the sarcomeres and ultimately producing muscle contraction.
- This process powers all types of movement: voluntary movements (e.g. walking, writing), involuntary movements (e.g. heart beating, peristalsis) and the act of breathing itself all require ATP.

### 2.2 Protein synthesis
- On ribosomes, amino acids scattered throughout the cytoplasm are joined together in a specific order, as instructed by mRNA, to form long polypeptide chains — an **energy‑consuming** process. The formation of each peptide bond requires ATP.
- Protein synthesis is the basis of cell growth, tissue repair and enzyme replacement.

### 2.3 Cell division
- Mitosis and meiosis both require cells to carry out a complex series of energy‑demanding steps: the accurate replication of DNA, the condensation of chromosomes, the assembly of the spindle, the separation of chromosomes and finally the division of the cytoplasm. All of these steps are driven by ATP.

### 2.4 Active transport
- Cells need to take up substances that are at a higher concentration inside the cell than outside (e.g. root hair cells absorbing nitrate ions from very dilute soil solutions), or to expel waste substances that are at a higher concentration outside the cell. This movement **against a concentration gradient** across membranes depends on carrier proteins and consumes ATP. Without active transport, cells could not obtain essential minerals or maintain their internal ion balance.

### 2.5 Growth
- Growth is not a single chemical reaction but the combined result of **the synthesis of new materials** and **an increase in the number of cells**.
- Synthesising complex organic molecules (such as polysaccharides, lipids, proteins and nucleic acids) requires energy. At the same time, increasing cell numbers through cell division also requires energy. Growth is therefore a process that is highly dependent on ATP.

### 2.6 Passage of nerve impulses
- Nerve cells need to maintain concentration differences of ions across their cell membranes: sodium ions (Na⁺) are more concentrated outside the cell, potassium ions (K⁺) are more concentrated inside. The maintenance of these gradients depends on the **sodium‑potassium pump**, which requires ATP.
- After a nerve impulse (an action potential) has passed, the cell must rapidly pump the ions back to their original positions to prepare for the next impulse. The huge amount of information processed in the brain and nervous system makes this one of the most energy‑expensive processes in the body.

### 2.7 Maintenance of a constant body temperature
- Mammals and birds are **endotherms**. A significant proportion of the energy released by respiration in organs such as muscles and the liver is given off as heat.
- This heat is used to maintain the core body temperature at around 37°C, thereby ensuring that the body’s enzymes always work at their optimum temperature. This stable, highly efficient metabolic environment makes complex life activities possible.

## 3. Aerobic Respiration

### 3.1 Definition
- **Aerobic respiration** is the form of cell respiration that takes place when **sufficient oxygen** is available. It breaks down glucose **completely**, oxidising it to simple inorganic substances (carbon dioxide and water), and **releases a large amount of energy**.
- “Complete oxidation” means that all the carbon–carbon and carbon–hydrogen bonds in the glucose molecule have been broken and all the extractable energy has been released.

### 3.2 Where it occurs — the mitochondrion
- Aerobic respiration takes place in **mitochondria**. A mitochondrion is an organelle bounded by a double membrane and is found in virtually all eukaryotic cells (except bacteria).
- The structure of a mitochondrion is highly specialised:
  - **Outer membrane**: smooth, separating the organelle from the cytoplasm.
  - **Inner membrane**: folded inwards to form **cristae**. This folding greatly increases the surface area for the attachment of respiratory enzymes, allowing many of the reactions of aerobic respiration to take place simultaneously.
  - **Matrix**: the fluid‑filled space enclosed by the inner membrane, containing circular DNA, ribosomes and the enzymes of the Krebs cycle.
- The more active a cell and the greater its demand for energy, the more mitochondria it contains. For example, heart muscle cells and sperm cells are packed with large numbers of mitochondria.

### 3.3 Equation(s)
- **Word equation**:

  **glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ a large amount of energy)**

- **Balanced chemical equation (Supplement)**:

  **C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O**

- This equation is the exact reverse of the **photosynthesis** equation, but it is crucial to understand that they are two separate sets of reactions that take place in different organelles and are catalysed by completely different enzymes. If an exam question asks for the “equation for respiration”, you must never write the equation for photosynthesis.

### 3.4 Efficiency
- Theoretically, the complete oxidation of one molecule of glucose through aerobic respiration can yield about **30–32 molecules of ATP** (some older textbooks quote 38 ATP, which is the theoretical maximum for the entire pathway).
- Regardless of the exact number, the key point is that the energy yield of aerobic respiration is far higher than that of anaerobic respiration.
- This is why the vast majority of organisms rely on aerobic respiration for most of their energy needs.

## 4. Anaerobic Respiration

### 4.1 Basic definition and features
- **Anaerobic respiration** is the form of cell respiration that takes place **in the absence of oxygen**. It breaks down glucose **incompletely**; the products are still organic molecules (lactic acid or ethanol), so only a small amount of energy is released.
- The two key features of anaerobic respiration must be remembered together: **(1) it does not require oxygen**; **(2) it releases much less energy than aerobic respiration**.
- Another important feature is that all the reactions of anaerobic respiration take place in the **cytoplasm (cytosol)** and have nothing to do with mitochondria.

### 4.2 Scenario 1: Anaerobic respiration in human muscles
- **Trigger**: During high‑intensity exercise such as sprinting or weightlifting, the oxygen demand of the muscles soars. The blood cannot deliver enough oxygen to the muscle cells in time. To meet the urgent energy demand, muscle cells temporarily switch to anaerobic respiration.
- **Word equation**:

  **glucose → lactic acid (+ a small amount of energy)**

- **Physiological effects of lactic acid**:
  - Lactic acid is an acidic substance. When it accumulates rapidly in the muscles and blood, it lowers the intracellular pH.
  - The low pH inhibits the activity of many enzymes in the muscle cells and interferes with the normal mechanism of muscle contraction, leading to feelings of **soreness** and **weakness**, and ultimately to **fatigue**.
  - Because lactic acid accumulation quickly impairs muscle function, vigorous exercise that depends on anaerobic respiration can only be sustained for a **very short time** (usually no more than one or two minutes).

### 4.3 Scenario 2: Anaerobic respiration in yeast (industrial fermentation)
- **Biological background**: Yeast is a single‑celled fungus. When oxygen is present, yeast preferentially carries out aerobic respiration, but under anaerobic conditions (e.g. inside dough or at the bottom of a fermentation vessel) it switches to anaerobic respiration.
- **Word equation**:

  **glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide (+ a small amount of energy)**

- **Balanced chemical equation (Supplement)**:

  **C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂**

- **Industrial applications**:
  1. **Bread making**: The **carbon dioxide** produced by anaerobic respiration of yeast in the dough becomes trapped in the gluten network, causing the dough to rise and become light and spongy. The **ethanol** evaporates during baking and does not affect the flavour of the bread.
  2. **Brewing**: In sealed fermentation vessels that exclude air, yeast converts the sugars in grape juice or malted barley into **ethanol**, producing wine, beer and other alcoholic beverages.
  3. **Biofuel**: Large‑scale fermentation of sugars from sugar cane or maize produces ethanol, which can be used as a renewable motor fuel.
- **Toxicity limitation**: Ethanol is itself toxic to yeast. When the ethanol concentration in the fermentation liquor reaches about 12–15%, the yeast is killed and fermentation stops naturally. This is why naturally fermented alcoholic drinks have a maximum alcohol content of around this level.

### 4.4 Comparison of energy yields
- Anaerobic respiration produces a net gain of only about **2 molecules of ATP** per molecule of glucose.
- Compared with aerobic respiration, the energy yield is only about **1/19**.
- This is because most of the energy in the glucose molecule remains locked in the chemical bonds of lactic acid or ethanol and is not released.

## 5. The Mechanism of Oxygen Debt

Oxygen debt is a central mechanism by which the body returns to its normal physiological state after vigorous exercise. It involves the coordinated work of the circulatory system, respiratory system and liver.

### 5.1 Definition of oxygen debt
- During vigorous exercise, muscles carry out anaerobic respiration because of a lack of oxygen, leading to an accumulation of lactic acid.
- **Oxygen debt** is defined as the **additional volume of oxygen** that must be taken in **after** exercise to oxidise the accumulated lactic acid completely.
- This can be thought of as oxygen that was “borrowed” during the exercise and must be “repaid” afterwards.

### 5.2 The process of repaying the oxygen debt
When exercise stops, the following three processes occur simultaneously until the lactic acid has been completely removed:

1. **Heart rate remains elevated**
   - The heart continues to beat rapidly, pumping blood quickly around the body.
   - This continually transports the lactic acid that has built up in the muscles to the **liver**.

2. **Breathing remains deep and rapid**
   - The lungs continue to ventilate at a high frequency, and the volume of air taken in with each breath (tidal volume) also increases.
   - This brings far more oxygen into the blood than at rest, supplying the liver with the oxygen needed for aerobic respiration of the lactic acid.

3. **The liver carries out aerobic respiration of lactic acid**
   - In liver cells, lactic acid is oxidised using oxygen.
   - Some of the lactic acid is completely broken down to **carbon dioxide and water**, releasing energy (some of which is used by the liver, the rest is given off as heat).
   - The remaining lactic acid is **converted back into glucose** (or stored as glycogen) through a series of energy‑requiring enzyme reactions. This glucose can then be transported back to the muscles to replenish their energy reserves.

This is why, after a sprint, even though you have stopped running, your heart rate and breathing remain rapid for some time — the body is actively repaying this “oxygen debt”. Only when the lactic acid has been cleared from the blood and all tissues have returned to their resting state can heart rate and breathing return to normal.

## 6. Core Comparison: Aerobic Respiration vs Anaerobic Respiration

The following comparison table is essential for clearly distinguishing between the two types of respiration.

| Feature | Aerobic respiration | Anaerobic respiration (animal muscle) | Anaerobic respiration (yeast) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxygen required?** | **Absolutely required** | **Not required** | **Not required** |
| **Site** | **Mitochondria** | **Cytoplasm** | **Cytoplasm** |
| **Degree of breakdown** | **Complete** | **Incomplete** | **Incomplete** |
| **End products** | Carbon dioxide + water | **Lactic acid** | **Ethanol + carbon dioxide** |
| **ATP yield per glucose molecule** | **High (about 30–32)** | **Low (only 2)** | **Low (only 2)** |
| **Effect on organism** | Provides continuous, efficient energy | Lactic acid accumulation causes muscle fatigue, limiting exercise duration | Ethanol accumulation is toxic to yeast, limiting fermentation concentration |
| **Typical situation** | Normal resting state, sustained exercise | Short bursts of intense exercise (e.g. sprinting, weightlifting) | Anaerobic fermentation environments (e.g. brewing, bread making)|
