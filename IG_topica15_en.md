## Part 2: Detailed Knowledge

### 15.1 Drugs

#### 15.1.1 What is a Drug?

A **drug** is any substance taken into the body that **modifies or affects chemical reactions** in the body.

Drugs can be:
- **Medicinal** – used to treat, prevent or diagnose diseases (e.g. antibiotics, painkillers)
- **Recreational** – taken for enjoyment or leisure rather than for medical reasons (e.g. alcohol, nicotine, caffeine)
- **Performance‑enhancing** – used to improve physical abilities (e.g. anabolic steroids)

Drugs work by interfering with normal biochemical pathways in cells. Most medicinal drugs are designed to target specific processes in pathogens or diseased cells without harming healthy body cells.

#### 15.1.2 Antibiotics – What They Are and How They Work

**Antibiotics** are drugs that kill bacteria or prevent their growth. They are used to treat **bacterial infections**.

**How antibiotics work:**
- Different antibiotics work in different ways. For example, some antibiotics (such as penicillin) interfere with the synthesis of the bacterial **cell wall**. Without a properly formed cell wall, bacteria absorb too much water by osmosis and burst (lyse).
- Other antibiotics inhibit protein synthesis in bacteria, preventing them from making essential enzymes and structural proteins.
- Because antibiotics target structures or processes that are specific to **bacterial cells** and are not present in human cells, they do **not** harm our own cells.

**Key point – antibiotics do NOT kill viruses:**
- Viruses do not have their own metabolic machinery – they use the host cell’s machinery to replicate. Antibiotics cannot target viruses because viruses do not possess the structures (such as cell walls or bacterial‑specific ribosomes) that antibiotics act upon.
- Treating a viral infection (such as influenza, the common cold or COVID‑19) with antibiotics is ineffective. This is a critical public health fact, and overuse of antibiotics for viral illnesses is a key driver of antibiotic resistance.

#### 15.1.3 Antibiotic Resistance

**What is antibiotic resistance?**
- Some bacteria have **resistance** to certain antibiotics, meaning that the antibiotic no longer kills them or stops their growth.
- Resistance occurs because of **genetic variation** within a population of bacteria. A few bacteria may already possess random **mutations** that make them less susceptible to a particular antibiotic.

**How antibiotic resistance develops:**
1. When a population of bacteria is exposed to an antibiotic, the **susceptible** (non‑resistant) bacteria die.
2. However, any bacteria that happen to have a mutation conferring resistance **survive** and reproduce.
3. These resistant bacteria pass on the resistance allele to their offspring (by binary fission).
4. Over time, the population of bacteria becomes dominated by the **antibiotic‑resistant strain**.
5. This is a classic example of **natural selection** – the antibiotic acts as the **selective agent**, giving a survival advantage to resistant bacteria.

**Consequences of antibiotic resistance:**
- Antibiotics that were once effective against certain bacteria become **less effective or completely ineffective**.
- This makes bacterial infections **harder to treat**, leading to longer illnesses, higher healthcare costs, and a greater risk of death from previously curable infections.
- Resistant strains of bacteria, such as **MRSA** (methicillin‑resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*), are now a serious problem worldwide.

#### 15.1.4 Limiting the Development of Resistant Bacteria (Supplement)

The development and spread of antibiotic resistance can be slowed by **using antibiotics only when essential**.

The key principle is that every time antibiotics are used, selection pressure is applied that favours resistant bacteria. Therefore, reducing **unnecessary antibiotic use** reduces the opportunity for resistant strains to develop and spread.

**Ways to limit development of antibiotic resistance:**
1. **Only take antibiotics when prescribed** by a doctor for a bacterial infection. Do not take antibiotics for viral infections (e.g. colds, flu).
2. **Complete the full course of antibiotics** – even if symptoms improve, stopping early can leave some bacteria alive, including those that may be partially resistant.
3. **Avoid using antibiotics in agriculture** as growth promoters for livestock. This practice has historically contributed to the rise of resistant bacteria that can spread to humans.
4. **Good hygiene and infection control** in hospitals and the community to reduce the spread of resistant strains.
5. **Research and development** of new antibiotics and alternative treatments.

**Example — MRSA:**
- MRSA is a strain of the bacterium *Staphylococcus aureus* that has become resistant to multiple antibiotics, including methicillin and often others.
- MRSA infections are particularly dangerous in hospitals, where patients may have weakened immune systems, surgical wounds, or invasive devices.
- Controlling MRSA requires strict hygiene (hand washing, sterilisation of equipment), isolation of infected patients, and using antibiotics cautiously.

## Part 3: Glossary (Key Terms Required by the Syllabus)

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Drug** | Any substance taken into the body that modifies or affects chemical reactions in the body. |
| **Antibiotic** | A drug that kills bacteria or prevents their growth; used to treat bacterial infections. |
| **Antibiotic resistance** | The ability of some bacteria to survive exposure to an antibiotic that would normally kill them or stop their growth. |
| **Mutation** | A random change in the DNA sequence of an organism. Mutations can give rise to new alleles, including alleles that confer antibiotic resistance. |
| **Natural selection** | The process by which organisms with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on those advantageous alleles to the next generation. |
| **MRSA** | Methicillin‑resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*; a strain of bacteria that has become resistant to many commonly used antibiotics. |
| **Selective agent** | An environmental factor (e.g. an antibiotic) that gives a survival advantage to individuals with a particular trait, driving natural selection. |

## Part 4: Common Exam Errors and Corrections

| Common Error | Correct Answer |
| :--- | :--- |
| Saying "antibiotics kill viruses" | Antibiotics kill **bacteria**, not viruses. Viruses are unaffected because they lack the structures that antibiotics target |
| Thinking resistance is caused by the antibiotic | Resistance is caused by **random mutations** that already exist in the bacterial population. The antibiotic simply selects for bacteria that have these mutations |
| Saying "a bacterium becomes immune to an antibiotic" | Use the term **resistant**, not "immune". Immunity is a specific term used for the vertebrate immune system |
| Writing "antibiotics work on the common cold" | The common cold is caused by a **virus**, so antibiotics are **ineffective** and should not be used |
| Stopping a course of antibiotics early "to save them for next time" | This is dangerous – always complete the full course to ensure all bacteria are killed, reducing the risk of resistance developing |
| Confusing MRSA with a virus | MRSA is a **bacterium**, not a virus. It is a strain of *Staphylococcus aureus* that is resistant to methicillin and other antibiotics |
| Not giving a reason for limiting antibiotic use | The key argument: reducing unnecessary use **reduces selection pressure**, so resistant strains are less likely to develop and spread |
