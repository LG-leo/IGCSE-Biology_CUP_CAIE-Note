## 📘 Topic 21: Biotechnology and Genetic Modification — Detailed Notes

---

### 21.1 Biotechnology and Genetic Modification

#### 🔹 Use of Bacteria in Biotechnology and Genetic Modification

**Core:**

- Bacteria are useful in biotechnology and genetic modification because of:
  - **Rapid reproduction rate**
  - **Ability to make complex molecules**

**Supplement:**

- Additional reasons why bacteria are useful:
  - **(a) Few ethical concerns over their manipulation and growth** — compared to animals or humans, there is less ethical concern about genetically modifying bacteria
  - **(b) The presence of plasmids** — plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules that can easily accept foreign genes, making genetic modification much easier

### 21.2 Biotechnology

#### 🔹 Anaerobic Respiration in Yeast — Ethanol Production (Biofuels)

- **Yeast** carries out **anaerobic respiration** under **anaerobic conditions**, converting sugar into **ethanol** and **carbon dioxide (CO₂)**.
- Ethanol can be used as a **biofuel** (e.g. bioethanol, which can be added to petrol as a vehicle fuel).

> Equation: glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide + energy (small amount)

#### 🔹 Anaerobic Respiration in Yeast — Bread-making

- In bread-making, yeast produces **carbon dioxide (CO₂)** through anaerobic respiration.
- CO₂ gas forms **bubbles** in the dough, causing the dough to **rise**.
- During baking, the ethanol evaporates and the bread sets.

#### 🔹 Use of Pectinase in Fruit Juice Production

- **Pectinase** is an enzyme used to break down **pectin** (a substance in plant cell walls that makes fruit juice cloudy).
- Using pectinase can:
  - **Increase juice yield** — more juice is released from the fruit
  - **Make the juice clearer** — breaking down pectin clarifies the juice

#### 🔹 Biological Washing Powders

- Biological washing powders contain **enzymes**, such as:
  - **Proteases** — break down protein stains (e.g. blood, egg, grass)
  - **Lipases** — break down fat/grease stains
- **Advantages**:
  - Work effectively at **lower temperatures** → saves energy and money
  - Effectively remove **organic stains**

#### 🔹 Use of Lactase to Produce Lactose-free Milk (Supplement)

- **Lactase** breaks down lactose (the sugar in milk) into **glucose** and **galactose**.
- Used to produce **lactose-free milk** for people who are **lactose intolerant**.
- Method: lactase is immobilised on alginate beads; as milk flows over the beads, the lactose is broken down.

#### 🔹 Fermenters — Large-scale Production (Supplement)

- **Fermenters** are containers used for **growing microorganisms on a large scale** (bacteria, fungi) to produce useful products.

**Conditions that need to be controlled in a fermenter:**

| Condition | Why it needs to be controlled |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **Temperature** | Microorganisms have an optimum growth temperature; too high will kill them, too low will slow growth |
| **pH** | Microorganisms have an optimum pH; deviation inhibits enzyme activity and growth |
| **Oxygen** | Aerobic microorganisms need oxygen for aerobic respiration; anaerobic microorganisms need oxygen-free conditions |
| **Nutrient supply** | Microorganisms need nutrients (sugar, nitrogen sources, etc.) for growth and product synthesis |
| **Waste products** | Accumulation of waste products inhibits microbial growth and needs to be removed regularly |

**Examples of products made using fermenters:**

| Product | Microorganism Used | Use |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| **Insulin** | Genetically modified bacteria | Treatment of diabetes |
| **Penicillin** | Penicillium fungus | Antibiotic, treats bacterial infections |
| **Mycoprotein** | Fungus | High-protein food (e.g. Quorn) |

### 21.3 Genetic Modification

#### 🔹 Definition (Core)

- **Genetic modification**: changing the genetic material of an organism by **removing, changing, or inserting individual genes**.
- Also known as **genetic engineering**.

#### 🔹 Examples of Genetic Modification (Core)

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **(a) Insertion of human genes into bacteria to produce human proteins** | e.g. inserting the human insulin gene into bacteria; bacteria produce human insulin for diabetes treatment |
| **(b) Insertion of genes into crop plants to confer resistance to herbicides** | Crops become resistant to a specific herbicide; farmers can spray herbicide to kill weeds without harming the crop |
| **(c) Insertion of genes into crop plants to confer resistance to insect pests** | Crops produce a protein toxic to insect pests, reducing the need for chemical insecticides |
| **(d) Insertion of genes into crop plants to improve nutritional qualities** | e.g. **Golden Rice** — genes inserted to produce beta-carotene (a precursor of vitamin A) |

#### 🔹 Process of Genetic Modification (Supplement)

Using **bacterial production of a human protein** as an example:

| Step | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **(a) Isolation of the human gene** | Use **restriction enzymes** to cut the DNA containing the human target gene, forming **sticky ends** |
| **(b) Cutting of bacterial plasmid DNA** | Use the **same restriction enzymes** to cut the bacterial plasmid DNA, forming **complementary sticky ends** |
| **(c) Insertion of human DNA** | Use **DNA ligase** to join the human DNA into the bacterial plasmid DNA, forming a **recombinant plasmid** |
| **(d) Insertion of recombinant plasmids into bacteria** | The recombinant plasmid is introduced into bacterial cells (specific details are not required) |
| **(e) Multiplication of bacteria** | Bacteria containing the recombinant plasmid **divide by mitosis**, producing many copies of the plasmid |
| **(f) Expression of the human gene** | Bacteria express the human gene and **produce the human protein** (e.g. insulin) |

**Process Summary:**

```
Human DNA → cut by restriction enzymes → sticky ends
Bacterial plasmid → cut by same restriction enzymes → complementary sticky ends
↓
Joined by DNA ligase → recombinant plasmid
↓
Inserted into bacteria → bacteria multiply → human protein produced
```

#### 🔹 Advantages and Disadvantages of Genetically Modified Crops (Supplement)

Using **soya, maize, and rice** as examples:

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------------|---------------|
| **Increased yield** — pest and herbicide resistance reduce crop losses | **Unknown long-term health effects** — safety of GM foods is still debated |
| **Reduced pesticide use** — pest-resistant crops need fewer chemical insecticides | **Possible allergic reactions** — new genes may introduce new allergens |
| **Improved nutritional value** — e.g. Golden Rice is rich in vitamin A | **Gene flow to wild relatives** — GM crops may hybridise with wild plants, creating "superweeds" |
| **Increased stress tolerance** — can develop drought-resistant or salt-tolerant crops | **Reduced biodiversity** — large-scale planting of single GM varieties reduces genetic diversity |
| **Lower production costs** — reduced need for pesticides and labour | **Ethical concerns** — some people believe genetic modification is "unnatural" |
| **Addressing food shortages** — helps feed the growing global population | **Corporate control of seed supply** — farmers may become dependent on large companies for seeds |
