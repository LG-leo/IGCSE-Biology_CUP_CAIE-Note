## 11.1 Features of Gas Exchange Surfaces

### 11.1.1 Four Key Features (Core)

Efficient gas exchange in the alveoli requires the following four features:

| Feature | Explanation | Exam application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Large surface area** | Millions of alveoli provide a total surface area of ~70 m². More space for simultaneous gas exchange. | Faster diffusion of O₂ and CO₂ |
| **Thin surface** | Alveolar wall is one cell thick; capillary wall is also one cell thick. Shortest diffusion distance. | Extremely fast diffusion |
| **Good blood supply** | Dense capillary network surrounds each alveolus, continuously carrying deoxygenated blood away. | Maintains steep concentration gradient |
| **Good ventilation** | Breathing constantly refreshes air in the alveoli. | Keeps O₂ high and CO₂ low in the alveolar air |

### 11.1.2 Structural Adaptations of Alveoli (Detailed)

| Adaptation | Feature | Function | Exam keywords |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Many alveoli** | Millions of tiny air sacs | Provide a huge surface area | large surface area → faster diffusion |
| **Thin walls** | Single layer of squamous epithelium, 0.1–0.5 µm thick | Extremely short diffusion path | short diffusion distance |
| **Moist surface** | Inner surface lined with a thin layer of fluid | Gases dissolve and diffuse more easily | gases dissolve → faster diffusion |
| **Rich capillary network** | Hundreds of capillaries around each alveolus | Continuously take away O₂ and bring CO₂ | maintains steep concentration gradient |
| **Ventilation** | Breathing movements renew air | Keeps alveolar O₂ high, CO₂ low | maintains concentration gradient |

### 11.1.3 Concentration Gradient & Good Blood Supply

- **O₂ gradient**: high in alveoli → low in capillary blood → O₂ diffuses into blood.
- **CO₂ gradient**: high in capillary blood → low in alveoli → CO₂ diffuses into alveoli.
- **Good blood supply** continuously removes oxygenated blood and brings deoxygenated blood, **maintaining the steep concentration gradient** for both gases.

> **Paper 4 template**: “Good blood supply maintains a steep concentration gradient, so oxygen diffuses rapidly into the blood and carbon dioxide diffuses rapidly out.”

## 11.2 Structure of the Breathing System

### 11.2.1 Parts and Functions (syllabus identification)

| Structure | Location / description | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lungs** | inside the thorax | main site of gas exchange |
| **Diaphragm** | dome‑shaped muscle below lungs | contracts → flattens → increases thoracic volume → inhalation |
| **Ribs** | surround the thorax | protect lungs and heart; assist breathing movements |
| **Intercostal muscles** | between ribs | external intercostals contract → lift ribs → increase volume |
| **Larynx** | top of trachea | voice production; air passage |
| **Trachea** | in front of oesophagus | air passage; supported by C‑shaped cartilage rings |
| **Bronchi** | two branches from trachea into each lung | air passage |
| **Bronchioles** | further branches of bronchi | distribute air to alveoli |
| **Alveoli** | tiny air sacs at ends of bronchioles | gas exchange |
| **Associated capillaries** | surround each alveolus | gas exchange |

> **Air pathway (memorise order)**: nasal/oral cavity → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli.

### 11.2.2 Cartilage in the Trachea (Supplement)

- **Function**: keeps the trachea **open** (prevents collapse) during inhalation when pressure in the thorax drops.
- Cartilage is **C‑shaped** (open at the back) allowing the oesophagus to expand during swallowing.

### 11.2.3 Thorax

The thorax is the chest cavity containing the ribs, intercostal muscles, diaphragm, trachea, lungs, and pleural membranes (reduce friction).

## 11.3 Ventilation Mechanism

Changes during inspiration (inhalation) and expiration (exhalation):

| Process | Diaphragm | External intercostal muscles | Ribs | Thoracic volume | Intrapulmonary pressure | Air flow |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Inhalation** | contracts → flattens | contract | lift up and out | **increases** | **decreases (below atmospheric)** | **in** |
| **Exhalation** | relaxes → domes up | relax | move down | **decreases** | **increases (above atmospheric)** | **out** |

> **Forced exhalation (Supplement)**: internal intercostal muscles contract, pulling ribs down further, reducing thoracic volume more → faster, harder exhalation.

## 11.4 Composition of Inspired and Expired Air

### 11.4.1 Differences Table

| Gas | Inspired air | Expired air | Reason |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxygen (O₂)** | ~21% | **~16%** | used for cellular respiration |
| **Carbon dioxide (CO₂)** | ~0.04% | **~4%** | waste product of respiration |
| **Water vapour (H₂O)** | low (variable) | **~1–5%** (saturated) | air is humidified in airways |
| **Nitrogen (N₂)** | ~78% | ~78% | inert, not used |

### 11.4.2 Differences in Composition Explained (Supplement)

- O₂ decreases because it is taken up by blood for cellular respiration.
- CO₂ increases because it is produced by cellular respiration and diffuses into alveoli.
- Water vapour increases because air is warmed and moistened by the respiratory tract.

### 11.4.3 Testing for CO₂ with Limewater

- **Reagent**: limewater (calcium hydroxide solution, Ca(OH)₂).
- **Method**: bubble exhaled air through limewater.
- **Positive result**: turns from colourless to **milky/cloudy** (due to formation of calcium carbonate precipitate).
- **Uses**: to compare CO₂ content of inhaled vs exhaled air (exhaled air produces more turbidity).

## 11.5 Effect of Physical Activity on Breathing

### 11.5.1 Observed Effects (Core)

| Effect | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Rate increases** | number of breaths per minute goes up |
| **Depth increases** | tidal volume (amount per breath) increases |
| **Recovery time** | takes time to return to resting after exercise |

### 11.5.2 Mechanism (Supplement – Paper 4 essential)

During exercise, muscle cells respire more → **more CO₂ produced** → CO₂ diffuses into blood → CO₂ combines with water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) → **blood pH decreases** (becomes slightly more acidic) → **chemoreceptors in the medulla** (brain) detect the change → send nerve impulses to **diaphragm and intercostal muscles** → muscles contract more **frequently and forcefully** → **rate and depth of breathing increase**.

> **Paper 4 template**: “Increased CO₂ → pH decrease → chemoreceptors in brain detect → impulses to diaphragm and intercostal muscles → rate and depth increase → more O₂ taken in, more CO₂ removed.”

## 11.6 Defence Mechanisms of the Respiratory System (Supplement)

| Structure | Location | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Goblet cells** | in trachea and bronchus epithelium | secrete **mucus** – traps dust, bacteria, viruses |
| **Mucus** | surface of airways | sticky fluid that traps inhaled particles and pathogens |
| **Ciliated cells (ciliated epithelium)** | lining of trachea and bronchi | have tiny **cilia** that beat rhythmically |

**Defence mechanism**:
- Goblet cells produce mucus that traps pathogens and particles.
- Cilia beat in a coordinated way, **moving mucus upward** towards the throat.
- Mucus is either **coughed out** or **swallowed** (stomach acid kills pathogens).
- This is the **mucus‑cilia escalator**, the first line of defence in airways.

## 11.7 Cholera (Supplement – syllabus requirement)

### 11.7.1 Causative Agent and Transmission
- **Pathogen**: *Vibrio cholerae* (a bacterium).
- **Transmission**: through **contaminated water** (drinking water or food contaminated with sewage).

### 11.7.2 Mechanism of Cholera Toxin (Paper 4 high frequency)
1. The cholera bacterium produces a **toxin** that acts on the epithelial cells of the small intestine.
2. The toxin stimulates the **secretion of chloride ions (Cl⁻)** into the lumen of the small intestine.
3. Increased Cl⁻ concentration in the lumen lowers water potential (makes it more negative).
4. Water moves from the blood/tissues into the intestinal lumen by **osmosis**.
5. Result: **profuse watery diarrhoea** (rice‑water stool), leading to **dehydration** and **loss of ions**.

### 11.7.3 Effects on the Body
- Massive water loss → decreased blood volume → low blood pressure, shock, possible death if not treated.
- Loss of ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) → electrolyte imbalance, affects nerve and muscle function.

### 11.7.4 Treatment and Prevention
- **Treatment**: oral rehydration solution (ORS) to replace water and electrolytes; intravenous fluids in severe cases.
- **Prevention**: clean drinking water, sanitation, sewage treatment, vaccination.

## 11.8 Effects of Smoking (extension / background knowledge)

| Disease / effect | Causing agent | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chronic bronchitis** | tar, smoke | chronic inflammation → goblet cell hyperplasia → excess mucus → persistent cough |
| **Emphysema** | particulates in smoke | inflammation → release of elastase by macrophages → breakdown of alveolar walls → reduced surface area → breathlessness |
| **Lung cancer** | carcinogens in tar (e.g. benzopyrene) | DNA mutation in lung cells → uncontrolled cell division → tumour |
| **Cardiovascular effects** | nicotine, carbon monoxide | CO binds to haemoglobin (irreversibly) reducing oxygen‑carrying capacity; nicotine causes vasoconstriction and raises blood pressure |

> *Note: The detailed effects of smoking are not required in the gas exchange syllabus for 2026–2028, but they are often tested in other topics (e.g. Topic 15 Drugs).*

## 📌 Common Exam Traps (Paper 2)

| Wrong idea | Correct statement |
| :--- | :--- |
| Respiration = gas exchange | Respiration = chemical reactions in cells; gas exchange = physical process |
| All arteries carry oxygenated blood | **Pulmonary artery** carries deoxygenated blood to lungs |
| All veins carry deoxygenated blood | **Pulmonary veins** carry oxygenated blood to left atrium |
| Trachea collapses easily | Trachea is held open by C‑shaped cartilage rings |
| Inspired air contains 0% CO₂ | Inspired air contains about **0.04% CO₂** |
| O₂ decreases because we breathe it out | O₂ is **used in cellular respiration** |
| CO₂ increases because inspired air contains it | CO₂ is a **waste product of respiration** |
| Exercise increases breathing due to lack of O₂ | Main driver is **increase in CO₂** causing pH decrease |
| Alveoli are dry | Alveoli are **moist** (helps gases dissolve) |
