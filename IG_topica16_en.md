## Part 2: Detailed Knowledge

### 16.1 Asexual Reproduction

#### 16.1.1 What is Asexual Reproduction? (Core)

Asexual reproduction is a process resulting in the production of **genetically identical offspring** (clones) from **one parent**. It does not involve gametes (sex cells) or fertilisation. Cell division occurs by **mitosis**, which produces exact copies of the parent cell's genetic material. The offspring have exactly the same genes as the parent – they are **clones** [reference:0].

Key features of asexual reproduction:
- Only **one parent** is involved [reference:1]
- No fusion of gametes
- No mixing of genetic information
- Offspring are **genetically identical** to the parent and to each other [reference:2]
- Cell division occurs by **mitosis** [reference:3]

#### 16.1.2 Examples of Asexual Reproduction (Core)

| Organism | Method of Asexual Reproduction | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Bacteria** | Binary fission | The bacterial cell's single circular DNA replicates; the cell then divides into two genetically identical daughter cells. Extremely rapid – bacteria can double every 20 minutes in ideal conditions [reference:4]. |
| **Yeast (fungus)** | Budding | A small bud grows off the side of the parent cell. As the bud enlarges, the nucleus of the parent divides and one daughter nucleus moves into the bud. The bud eventually pinches off to form a new, genetically identical yeast cell [reference:5]. |
| **Hydra** | Budding | A miniature hydra grows as a bud from the body wall of the parent. When sufficiently developed, it detaches and lives independently. |
| **Strawberry plants** | Runners (stolons) | Horizontal stems grow above ground from the parent plant. At nodes along the runner, new roots and shoots develop, forming a new plant that is genetically identical to the parent. Eventually the runner degrades, leaving the offspring independent [reference:6]. |
| **Potato plants** | Tubers | Underground stems swell to form tubers. Each tuber contains stored starch and buds ("eyes"). Each eye can sprout and grow into a new, genetically identical potato plant [reference:7]. |
| **Onions / Garlic** | Bulbs | A bulb is a short underground stem surrounded by swollen leaf bases full of stored food. New bulbs develop from the parent bulb's base, each capable of producing a genetically identical plant [reference:8]. |

#### 16.1.3 Advantages and Disadvantages of Asexual Reproduction (Supplement)

**(a) To a population of a species in the wild** [reference:9]:

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- |
| Only one parent needed – no need to find a mate | No genetic variation – all offspring are clones, exactly alike |
| Can reproduce quickly and efficiently | All individuals are equally vulnerable to the same disease or environmental change |
| Rapid colonisation of suitable habitats – a single individual can start a whole population | Cannot adapt to new or changing conditions – if the environment changes unfavourably, the entire population could be wiped out |
| Successful traits are passed on unchanged | A single harmful mutation affects all offspring equally |

**(b) To crop production (agriculture)** [reference:10]:

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- |
| Farmers can grow genetically identical plants with desired traits (e.g. taste, size, yield) | Crops are genetically identical – no resistance to new diseases |
| Uniformity in quality and appearance – makes harvesting, grading and marketing easier | Over‑reliance on one variety creates risk of entire crop failure if a disease strikes |
| Faster reproduction of high‑yield varieties | Cannot develop new traits through natural variation |
| Useful for reproducing seedless fruits (e.g. bananas, seedless grapes) | Reduces biodiversity in farming systems |

### 16.2 Sexual Reproduction

#### 16.2.1 What is Sexual Reproduction? (Core)

Sexual reproduction is a process involving the **fusion of the nuclei of two gametes** (sex cells) to form a **zygote** (fertilised egg cell) and the production of offspring that are **genetically different** from each other [reference:11].

- **Fertilisation** is defined as the fusion of the nuclei of gametes [reference:12].
- Sexual reproduction requires **two parents**.
- Gametes are produced by **meiosis** – a type of cell division that halves the chromosome number.
- Because meiosis creates gametes with different combinations of alleles, offspring are genetically different from each other and from either parent.
- Genetic variation arises through: (1) random assortment of chromosomes during meiosis, (2) crossing over between homologous chromosomes during meiosis, (3) random mating, and (4) random fertilisation (which sperm fertilises which egg).

#### 16.2.2 Haploid and Diploid Nuclei (Supplement)

- A **haploid nucleus** is a nucleus containing a **single set** of chromosomes. In humans, haploid cells contain 23 chromosomes. **Gametes** (sperm and egg cells) are haploid [reference:13].
- A **diploid nucleus** is a nucleus containing **two sets** of chromosomes. In humans, diploid cells contain 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). The **zygote** formed by fertilisation is diploid – it receives one set of chromosomes from each parent, restoring the full chromosome number.

#### 16.2.3 Advantages and Disadvantages of Sexual Reproduction (Supplement)

**(a) To a population of a species in the wild**:

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- |
| Produces **genetic variation** within the population – offspring are all different | Requires two parents – a mate must be found |
| Genetic variation provides the raw material for **natural selection** and adaptation to changing environments | Slower than asexual reproduction |
| Some individuals are more likely to survive if a disease strikes or the environment changes | Growth and development take longer |
| Prevents the accumulation of harmful recessive mutations | Energy‑expensive (requires meiosis, gamete production, mating, etc.) |

**(b) To crop production**:

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- |
| Genetic variation allows selective breeding of new varieties with improved traits (disease resistance, higher yield, better quality) | Offspring are not identical to the parent – uniform quality cannot be guaranteed |
| Populations can adapt to evolving pests and diseases | Requires more time and resources than asexual propagation |
| Greater genetic diversity provides resilience against environmental changes | Some offspring may have undesirable traits due to random genetic recombination |

### 16.3 Sexual Reproduction in Plants

#### 16.3.1 Structure of an Insect‑Pollinated Flower (Core)

You must be able to **identify in diagrams and images, and draw** the following structures [reference:14].

**Male reproductive parts (Stamen):**

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Filament** | A stalk that supports and holds up the anther, positioning it where visiting insects will brush against it [reference:15] |
| **Anther** | Produces and contains **pollen grains** (male gametes). The anther opens (dehisces) when pollen is mature to release the grains [reference:16] |

**Female reproductive parts (Carpel / Pistil):**

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Stigma** | A sticky surface at the top of the carpel that **captures and holds pollen grains** during pollination. The stickiness is often due to a sugary secretion [reference:17] |
| **Style** | A stalk connecting the stigma to the ovary. It supports the stigma and the **pollen tube grows down through it** from a germinated pollen grain on the stigma [reference:18] |
| **Ovary** | The hollow chamber at the base of the carpel containing **ovules**. After fertilisation, the ovary develops into the **fruit** [reference:19] |
| **Ovules** | Located inside the ovary. Each ovule contains a female gamete (egg cell nucleus). After fertilisation, each ovule develops into a **seed** [reference:20] |

**Other structures:**

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sepals** | Green, leaf‑like structures that enclose and **protect the developing flower bud** before it opens [reference:21] |
| **Petals** | Often **brightly coloured** and sometimes **scented** to attract insect pollinators. They may also have **nectar guides** (patterns visible to insects) to direct pollinators to the nectar and the reproductive structures [reference:22] |
| **Nectary** | A gland at the base of the ovary or petals that produces **nectar** – a sugary fluid that rewards visiting insects for pollination |

**Diagram reference**: The male part is the **stamen** (= filament + anther). The female part is the **carpel/pistil** (= stigma + style + ovary + ovules).

#### 16.3.2 Wind‑Pollinated Flowers (Core)

Wind‑pollinated flowers look very different from insect‑pollinated flowers. You must be able to identify and describe **anthers and stigmas** of a wind‑pollinated flower [reference:23][reference:24].

**Features of wind‑pollinated flowers:**

| Feature | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Petals** | Small, dull‑coloured (often green or brown), or absent – no need to attract insects |
| **Nectar and scent** | Absent – no reward is offered |
| **Anthers** | Large, loosely attached, and hang **outside** the flower on long, flexible filaments so that the wind can easily shake out the pollen |
| **Stigma** | Large, **feathery or brush‑like**, and hangs **outside** the flower to provide a large surface area to catch wind‑blown pollen from the air |
| **Pollen** | Produced in **huge quantities** because much of it is wasted (it is transported by random wind, not directed by an insect). Pollen grains are **small, light, smooth and dry** so they can be carried easily by the wind |

#### 16.3.3 Comparing Insect‑Pollinated vs Wind‑Pollinated Flowers (Core)

| Feature | Insect‑Pollinated | Wind‑Pollinated |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Petals** | Large, brightly coloured, often scented, sometimes with nectar guides | Small, green/brown, dull, sometimes absent |
| **Nectar** | Present – rewards insects | Absent |
| **Scent** | Often present | Absent |
| **Anthers** | Firmly attached, positioned inside the flower where insects brush against them | Large, loosely attached, hang outside the flower on long flexible filaments |
| **Stigma** | Small, sticky, enclosed inside the flower | Large, feathery, hanging outside the flower |
| **Pollen amount** | Moderate | Very large quantities |
| **Pollen grains** | Larger, sticky, spiky or sculpted to attach to insects' bodies | Small, light, smooth, dry – easily carried by wind |
| **Examples** | Rose, sweet pea, foxglove, buttercup, sunflower | Grasses, maize, wheat, oak, birch |

#### 16.3.4 Pollination (Core)

**Pollination** is the transfer of pollen grains from the **anther** (male part) to the **stigma** (female part) of a flower [reference:25].

**Self‑pollination (Supplement)**: the transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower **to the stigma of the same flower** or to the stigma of **a different flower on the same plant** [reference:26].

**Cross‑pollination (Supplement)**: the transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower **to the stigma of a flower on a different plant of the same species** [reference:27].

**Comparing self‑pollination and cross‑pollination (Supplement)**:

| Feature | Self‑Pollination | Cross‑Pollination |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Genetic variation** | Low – offspring are more similar to the parent | High – offspring are genetically different |
| **Adaptation to environmental change** | Poor – less likely to survive if environment changes | Good – some individuals may have traits suited to new conditions |
| **Reliance on pollinators** | Low – can occur even in the absence of insects or wind | High – depends on pollinators or wind to transfer pollen between plants |
| **Colonisation** | Good for isolated populations where pollinators are scarce | Requires proximity to other plants of the same species |
| **Risk of harmful recessive traits** | Higher – detrimental alleles may accumulate | Lower – harmful alleles can be masked by dominant alleles from the other parent |

#### 16.3.5 Fertilisation in Plants (Core & Supplement)

**Core**: Fertilisation occurs when a **pollen nucleus** (male gamete) fuses with a **nucleus in an ovule** (female gamete) [reference:28].

**Process of fertilisation in plants (Supplement)** [reference:29]:

1. **Pollination** – a pollen grain lands on the stigma.
2. **Germination** – the pollen grain absorbs sugar solution from the stigma and germinates, producing a **pollen tube**.
3. **Pollen tube growth** – the pollen tube grows **down the style** towards the ovary. The tube secretes enzymes to digest its way through the style tissue. The pollen tube nucleus stays at the tip, directing growth.
4. **Entry into the ovule** – the pollen tube enters the ovary and reaches a tiny opening (the micropyle) at the end of an ovule.
5. **Fertilisation** – the tip of the pollen tube breaks, releasing the **male nucleus**. This nucleus travels down the pollen tube and **fuses with the female nucleus** inside the ovule, forming a **zygote**.
6. **Post‑fertilisation changes** – The zygote develops into an **embryo plant** within the seed. The ovule develops into a **seed**. The ovary develops into a **fruit** (which encloses the seeds and aids their dispersal). Other flower parts (sepals, petals, stamens, stigma, style) wither and fall off.

#### 16.3.6 Seed Germination (Core)

Germination is the process by which a seed begins to grow and develop into a new plant. The embryo inside the seed breaks dormancy and resumes growth when conditions are favourable.

The three environmental conditions required for seed germination are **water, oxygen, and a suitable temperature** [reference:30].

| Condition | Why it is needed |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Water** | Needed to **activate enzymes** that break down stored food reserves (starch to glucose) in the cotyledons (seed leaves). Water also allows the seed to swell and the seed coat to split. Water transports dissolved substances within the growing embryo. |
| **Oxygen** | Needed for **aerobic respiration**, which releases energy (ATP) for cell division and growth. The growing embryo has a very high respiration rate and requires a continuous supply of oxygen. |
| **Suitable temperature** | A temperature warm enough to provide **optimum conditions for enzymes** involved in respiration and growth. Too cold = enzymes work too slowly; too hot = enzymes denature. Most seeds germinate well at around 20–25°C. |

**Note**: Light is generally **not required** for germination (seeds germinate underground where there is no light). However, some seeds do need light to break dormancy. The syllabus does not require knowledge of light effects.

### 16.4 Sexual Reproduction in Humans

#### 16.4.1 The Male Reproductive System (Core)

You must be able to **identify on diagrams** and **state the functions** of these structures [reference:31].

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Testes** (singular: testis) | Produce **sperm cells** (male gametes) continuously from puberty onwards. Also produce the hormone **testosterone**. Located outside the body in the scrotum [reference:32]. |
| **Scrotum** | A sac of skin that holds the testes **outside the body**. This keeps the testes at a temperature **slightly lower than body temperature** (around 34–35°C), which is essential for normal sperm production. Sperm production is impaired at core body temperature (37°C) [reference:33]. |
| **Sperm ducts** (vas deferens) | Muscular tubes that carry sperm from each testis, up into the abdomen and towards the urethra. During ejaculation, waves of peristaltic contraction push sperm along [reference:34]. |
| **Prostate gland** | Produces a nutrient‑rich fluid that mixes with sperm cells to form **semen**. This fluid contains enzymes and sugars that nourish sperm and help them swim and survive. The prostate fluid also helps neutralise the acidity of any urine remaining in the urethra and the acidity of the female vagina [reference:35]. |
| **Urethra** | A tube running down the centre of the penis. It carries **either** semen (during ejaculation) **or** urine (during urination) out of the body. A ring of muscle at the base of the urethra prevents semen and urine from mixing – they are never released simultaneously [reference:36]. |
| **Penis** | The external male organ that **transfers sperm into the vagina** during sexual intercourse. It contains erectile tissue which fills with blood to become erect, enabling penetration [reference:37]. |

**Note**: Testes = sperm + testosterone. The prostate adds fluid to sperm → forms semen. Urethra = tube for either sperm or urine (never both at the same time) [reference:38].

#### 16.4.2 The Female Reproductive System (Core)

You must be able to **identify on diagrams** and **state the functions** of these structures [reference:39].

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Ovaries** (singular: ovary) | Produce **egg cells (ova)** – the female gametes. Females are born with all their immature eggs already present in the ovaries. At puberty, eggs begin to mature (usually one per menstrual cycle). The ovaries also secrete the female sex hormones **oestrogen** and **progesterone** [reference:40]. |
| **Oviducts** (Fallopian tubes) | Tubes connecting each ovary to the uterus. The open, funnel‑shaped end near the ovary captures the released egg. Lined with **ciliated cells** that beat to create a current wafting the egg along the oviduct towards the uterus. **Fertilisation normally occurs in the oviduct** [reference:41][reference:42]. |
| **Uterus** (womb) | A hollow, muscular organ with a soft, spongy lining (endometrium). It is where a fertilised egg (zygote) **implants** and develops into an embryo and then a foetus during pregnancy. If fertilisation does not occur, the uterine lining breaks down and is shed during menstruation [reference:43][reference:44]. |
| **Cervix** | A ring of muscle at the lower end of the uterus, forming the entrance to the uterus from the vagina. It keeps the developing foetus in place during pregnancy and secretes a plug of mucus during pregnancy to protect against infection. During childbirth, the cervix **dilates** (widens) to allow the baby to pass through [reference:45]. |
| **Vagina** | A muscular tube leading from the cervix to the outside of the body. It **receives the penis and sperm** during sexual intercourse. It also acts as the **birth canal** during delivery of the baby and serves as the passageway for menstrual flow [reference:46]. |

**Note**: Ovaries = eggs + hormones (oestrogen and progesterone). Fertilisation → oviduct. Uterus = foetus development. Vagina = receives sperm + birth canal [reference:47].

#### 16.4.3 Fertilisation in Humans (Core)

**Fertilisation** is the fusion of the **nuclei** from a male gamete (sperm cell) and a female gamete (egg cell) [reference:48].

- Fertilisation usually occurs in the **oviduct** (often in the upper third, closest to the ovary) [reference:49][reference:50].
- During sexual intercourse, millions of sperm are deposited in the vagina. They swim through the cervix, into the uterus, and up into the oviducts.
- Only one sperm cell successfully penetrates the egg cell membrane. Once it enters, the egg's surface changes (the jelly coat hardens) so no more sperm can enter [reference:51].
- The sperm nucleus fuses with the egg nucleus, forming a **zygote**.
- The zygote is **diploid** – it contains a full set of chromosomes (46 in humans), half from the mother and half from the father.

#### 16.4.4 Adaptive Features of Gametes (Core)

**Sperm cell adaptations** [reference:52][reference:53]:

| Adaptation | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Flagellum (tail)** | A long, whip‑like tail that beats to propel the sperm, allowing it to **swim towards the egg** through the female reproductive tract |
| **Many mitochondria** | Located in the mid‑piece, just behind the head. They release energy (ATP) through aerobic respiration to power the beating of the flagellum |
| **Enzymes in the acrosome** (head region) | The acrosome is a cap‑like structure at the tip of the sperm head containing **digestive enzymes**. When the sperm reaches the egg, these enzymes are released to **digest a pathway through the jelly coat** surrounding the egg, enabling the sperm to penetrate and fertilise it |
| **Streamlined head** | Reduces resistance as the sperm swims |

**Egg cell (ovum) adaptations** [reference:54][reference:55]:

| Adaptation | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cytoplasm containing energy stores** (food reserves) | Provides nutrients and energy for the developing zygote after fertilisation, supplying it as it repeatedly divides while travelling along the oviduct |
| **Jelly‑like coating** | A glycoprotein layer (the zona pellucida) outside the cell membrane that **changes after fertilisation** to become impermeable to other sperm, preventing polyspermy (multiple sperm fertilising the same egg) |

#### 16.4.5 Comparison of Male and Female Gametes (Core)

| Feature | Sperm | Egg (Ovum) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Size** | Very small (~45 µm long) – among the smallest cells in the human body | Large (~0.15 mm diameter) – just visible to the naked eye. The largest cell in the human body |
| **Structure** | Highly specialised: head region (nucleus + acrosome), mid‑piece (mitochondria), flagellum | Round, relatively unspecialised, surrounded by a jelly‑like coating |
| **Motility** | Capable of locomotion – actively swims using its flagellum | Not capable of locomotion – moved passively by cilia in the oviduct and by contractions of the oviduct wall |
| **Numbers produced** | Produced in **huge numbers** every day after puberty (~100‑300 million released per ejaculation) | Thousands of immature eggs in each ovary, but usually only **one** matures and is released per menstrual cycle (ovulation). Only around 400–500 are ovulated over a woman's reproductive lifetime |

#### 16.4.6 Early Development: Zygote to Implantation (Core)

After fertilisation in the oviduct:

1. The **zygote** undergoes repeated cell divisions by **mitosis** as it travels down the oviduct towards the uterus (this journey takes approximately 3 days).
2. It becomes a solid ball of cells called an **embryo** [reference:56].
3. The embryo reaches the uterus and **implants into the thick, spongy lining** (endometrium) of the uterus wall.
4. After implantation, the embryo continues to grow and develop. A structure called the **placenta** forms at the site of implantation, connecting the developing embryo to the mother's blood supply.

#### 16.4.7 Development of the Foetus: Key Structures (Core)

The syllabus requires you to identify and state the functions of the following structures in the development of the foetus [reference:57].

| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Placenta** | A temporary organ that develops from the embryo's outer cells and the mother's uterine lining. It is the site of **exchange of materials** between the mother's blood and the foetus's blood. The mother and foetus's blood supplies are extremely close but **never mix**. The placenta delivers oxygen and dissolved nutrients (glucose, amino acids, vitamins, minerals, antibodies) from the mother to the foetus. It removes waste products (carbon dioxide, urea) from the foetus's blood into the mother's blood. The mother's body then excretes these wastes. The placenta also produces the hormone **progesterone** to maintain the pregnancy [reference:58][reference:59][reference:60]. |
| **Umbilical cord** | The cord that **physically connects the foetus to the placenta**. It contains the umbilical artery and umbilical vein – blood vessels that carry the foetus's blood to and from the placenta. There are no nerves in the umbilical cord, so cutting it after birth is not painful [reference:61]. |
| **Amniotic sac** (amnion) | A tough membrane that surrounds and encloses the developing embryo/foetus, forming a sealed, fluid‑filled bag |
| **Amniotic fluid** | The fluid contained within the amniotic sac, made mainly from the mother's blood plasma. It **cushions the developing foetus from physical bumps and jolts** to the mother's abdomen, **allows the foetus to move freely** (promoting muscle and bone development), **maintains a constant temperature** around the foetus, and acts as a **barrier to infection** [reference:62] |

**Key point to remember**: The placenta allows EXCHANGE of substances – oxygen, glucose, amino acids cross from mother to foetus; carbon dioxide and urea cross from foetus to mother. The two blood supplies are very close but **do not mix** [reference:63][reference:64].

#### 16.4.8 The Placenta as an Exchange Organ and Its Limitations (Supplement)

The placenta is adapted for efficient exchange of materials:

- **Large surface area** for diffusion (the chorionic villi of the foetal part of the placenta greatly increase the surface area)
- **Thin barrier** between maternal and foetal blood (only a few cells thick)

Materials that cross from **mother → foetus**: oxygen, glucose, amino acids, vitamins, minerals, antibodies (IgG), water

Materials that cross from **foetus → mother**: carbon dioxide, urea (waste products)

Transport mechanisms: most substances cross by **diffusion** (down their concentration gradients); some (e.g. amino acids) are actively transported for more efficient transfer.

**Barrier function**: The placenta normally acts as a selective barrier. However, **some pathogens and toxins can cross** the placenta and affect the foetus [reference:65]:

- **Pathogens that can cross**: rubella (German measles) virus, HIV, Zika virus, *Toxoplasma*
- **Toxins that can cross**: nicotine (from cigarette smoke), alcohol, certain drugs (e.g. heroin, cocaine, thalidomide)
- This is why pregnant women are advised not to smoke (nicotine reduces oxygen supply to the foetus and can cause low birth weight), not to drink alcohol (alcohol can cause foetal alcohol syndrome, leading to brain damage and facial abnormalities), and to avoid certain medications during pregnancy [reference:66][reference:67].

### 16.5 Sex Hormones in Humans

#### 16.5.1 Hormones and Puberty (Core)

**Puberty** is the stage in human development when **secondary sexual characteristics** begin to appear. This occurs under the control of **sex hormones** [reference:68].

**Primary sexual characteristics** are the reproductive organs present at birth (e.g. penis and testes in males; ovaries and uterus in females).

**Secondary sexual characteristics** are the physical changes that occur during puberty as children become adolescents. They are controlled by the release of hormones: **oestrogen** in girls and **testosterone** in boys [reference:69].

**Testosterone** (male sex hormone):
- **Produced by**: The **testes**
- **Main role**: Controls the development of male secondary sexual characteristics during puberty
- **Effects**: stimulates sperm production; causes deepening of the voice; growth of facial, pubic and body hair; increase in muscle mass and body size; growth of the penis and testes [reference:70]

**Oestrogen** (female sex hormone):
- **Produced by**: The **ovaries**
- **Main role**: Controls the development of female secondary sexual characteristics and regulates the menstrual cycle
- **Effects**: stimulates development of breasts; starts the menstrual cycle; growth of pubic and underarm hair; widening of the hips; development of fat distribution in hips and thighs [reference:71]

Some changes occur to both sexes during puberty: growth of sexual organs, growth of body hair, and emotional changes (increased interest in sex, mood swings) [reference:72].

#### 16.5.2 The Menstrual Cycle (Core)

The **menstrual cycle** is a roughly **28‑day** (average) monthly process involving hormonal changes, ovulation, and preparation of the uterus for possible pregnancy. It begins at puberty (around age 12) and continues until menopause (around age 45–55) [reference:73][reference:74].

The menstrual cycle is described in terms of **changes in the ovaries** and **changes in the lining of the uterus** [reference:75].

| Day Range | Changes in the Ovaries | Changes in the Uterus Lining (Endometrium) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Day 1–5** (Menstruation) | The ovary is at rest; low hormone levels | The uterine lining **breaks down and is shed** as menstrual blood through the vagina (commonly called a "period") [reference:76] |
| **Day 6–13** (Follicular phase) | A **follicle** (a sac containing an immature egg) develops in the ovary. The follicle produces oestrogen [reference:77] | The lining of the uterus **begins to thicken** again in response to oestrogen – becomes spongy and rich in blood vessels to prepare for possible implantation [reference:78] |
| **Day 14** (Ovulation) | **Ovulation**: a mature egg is released from the ovary. The egg is swept into the oviduct by the funnel‑shaped end of the oviduct [reference:79] | The lining is now **thick and rich in blood vessels**, ready to receive a fertilised egg (zygote) [reference:80] |
| **Day 15–28** (Luteal phase) | The empty follicle (after the egg has been released) becomes the **corpus luteum** (Latin for "yellow body"). The corpus luteum secretes progesterone [reference:81]. | The lining **remains thick** and well‑supplied with blood under the influence of progesterone, ready for implantation [reference:82]. If no fertilisation occurs, the corpus luteum breaks down (usually around day 26–28), progesterone levels drop, and the lining is no longer maintained → this triggers the start of a new cycle (Day 1: menstruation). |

**If fertilisation occurs**: The zygote implants into the thick uterine lining. The developing embryo produces a hormone (hCG – human chorionic gonadotropin) that keeps the corpus luteum alive, so progesterone levels stay high and the lining is maintained. No menstruation occurs – this is the earliest sign of pregnancy.

**If fertilisation does NOT occur**: The corpus luteum breaks down. Hormone levels fall. The uterine lining is shed → menstruation begins, and the cycle restarts [reference:83].

#### 16.5.3 Hormonal Control of the Menstrual Cycle (Supplement)

The menstrual cycle is controlled by four hormones. You must know their **sites of production** and their **roles** [reference:84].

| Hormone | Where produced | Main roles |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **FSH** (Follicle‑Stimulating Hormone) | **Pituitary gland** (in the brain) | (1) Stimulates the **development of a follicle** in the ovary. (2) Stimulates the developing follicle to produce **oestrogen** [reference:85][reference:86] |
| **LH** (Luteinising Hormone) | **Pituitary gland** (in the brain) | (1) Triggers **ovulation** (release of the mature egg) around Day 14. (2) Stimulates the empty follicle to develop into the **corpus luteum**, which produces progesterone. LH is released as a large surge triggered by a peak in oestrogen [reference:87][reference:88] |
| **Oestrogen** | **Developing follicle** in the ovary (during the menstrual cycle); **placenta** (during pregnancy) | (1) Stimulates **thickening** and repair of the uterine lining after menstruation. (2) **Inhibits further FSH secretion** (negative feedback – prevents more follicles from developing in the same cycle). (3) At a high level just before ovulation, oestrogen **stimulates LH release** (positive feedback), triggering ovulation |
| **Progesterone** | **Corpus luteum** in the ovary (during the menstrual cycle); **placenta** (during pregnancy) | (1) **Maintains the thick uterine lining** throughout the second half of the cycle (days 14–28) so that a fertilised egg can implant. (2) **Inhibits FSH and LH** (negative feedback – prevents ovulation during pregnancy) [reference:89] |

The four hormones interact in a tightly regulated sequence:

1. **FSH** is released from the pituitary → stimulates **follicle development**.
2. The developing **follicle secretes oestrogen** → uterine lining thickens.
3. Oestrogen rises to a peak → triggers a **surge in LH** → **ovulation** (day 14).
4. The ruptured follicle becomes the **corpus luteum** and secretes **progesterone** → maintains the uterine lining [reference:90].
5. If pregnancy occurs, the corpus luteum continues to produce progesterone until the placenta takes over.
6. If no pregnancy, corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone falls → **menstruation**.

### 16.6 Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

#### 16.6.1 What is a Sexually Transmitted Infection? (Core)

A **sexually transmitted infection (STI)** is an infection that is transmitted through **sexual contact** (vaginal, anal or oral intercourse). The pathogen passes from an infected person to an uninfected person during sexual activity [reference:91].

#### 16.6.2 HIV and AIDS (Core)

- **HIV** (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a **pathogen** (a virus) that causes an STI [reference:92].
- HIV infection may lead to **AIDS** (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome) if left untreated [reference:93]. AIDS is the final, most severe stage of HIV infection, when the immune system is so badly damaged that the person becomes vulnerable to opportunistic infections and certain cancers that a healthy immune system would normally control.

**How HIV works**: HIV infects and destroys **helper T‑lymphocytes** (a type of white blood cell essential for coordinating the immune response). As the number of these cells declines, the immune system progressively weakens, leaving the body unable to fight off infections that would normally be harmless.

**Methods of HIV transmission** [reference:94]:
- **Unprotected sexual intercourse** with an infected person (the most common mode)
- **Sharing needles or syringes** contaminated with infected blood (e.g. intravenous drug use)
- **From mother to child** – across the placenta during pregnancy, during childbirth (contact with infected blood and fluids), or through breast milk during breastfeeding
- **Blood transfusions** with infected blood (now extremely rare in countries where donated blood is screened)
- **Contaminated surgical or dental equipment** (extremely rare where proper sterilisation is practised)

**Note**: HIV is **NOT** transmitted by casual contact such as hugging, shaking hands, sharing food or drink, using the same toilet seat, mosquito bites, or kissing (unless both people have open mouth sores or bleeding gums).

#### 16.6.3 Controlling the Spread of STIs (Core)

The spread of STIs can be controlled through [reference:95]:

1. **Using condoms** (barrier methods) during sexual intercourse – condoms prevent direct contact with infected body fluids (semen, vaginal fluid, blood) and are highly effective when used correctly and consistently.
2. **Limiting the number of sexual partners** – reducing the number of partners reduces the chance of encountering an infected individual.
3. **Regular STI testing and treatment** – early detection and treatment of STIs prevents further transmission.
4. **Education and awareness** – teaching people about the risks of STIs, how they are transmitted and how to protect themselves.
5. **Not sharing needles** – providing clean needle‑exchange programmes for injecting drug users.
6. **Screening donated blood** for HIV and other blood‑borne pathogens.
7. **Antiretroviral therapy (ART)** for HIV‑positive individuals – reduces the viral load to undetectable levels, which dramatically reduces the risk of onward transmission.
8. **Prevention of mother‑to‑child transmission** – providing antiretroviral drugs to HIV‑positive pregnant women reduces the risk of transmitting HIV to the baby during pregnancy, childbirth or breastfeeding.

## Part 3: Glossary (Key Terms Required by the Syllabus)

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Asexual reproduction** | A process resulting in the production of genetically identical offspring from one parent, without the involvement of gametes or fertilisation. |
| **Clone** | An organism or cell that is genetically identical to the parent organism from which it was produced. |
| **Sexual reproduction** | A process involving the fusion of the nuclei of two gametes (sex cells) to form a zygote, producing offspring that are genetically different from each other. |
| **Gamete** | A sex cell (sperm in males, egg/ovum in females). Gametes are haploid – they contain half the normal number of chromosomes. |
| **Fertilisation** | The fusion of the nuclei of a male gamete and a female gamete to form a zygote. |
| **Zygote** | A diploid cell formed by the fusion of two haploid gametes during fertilisation. It is the first cell of a new organism. |
| **Haploid** | A nucleus containing a single set of chromosomes (n). Gametes are haploid. Human haploid number = 23. |
| **Diploid** | A nucleus containing two sets of chromosomes (2n). Zygotes and body cells are diploid. Human diploid number = 46 (23 pairs). |
| **Pollination** | The transfer of pollen grains from the anther (male part) to the stigma (female part) of a flower. |
| **Self‑pollination** | The transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same flower, or to a different flower on the same plant. |
| **Cross‑pollination** | The transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower to the stigma of a flower on a different plant of the same species. |
| **Embryo** (plant) | The developing plant contained within a seed, formed from the zygote after fertilisation. |
| **Embryo** (human) | The early stage of development in humans, from the first division of the zygote until about 8 weeks; after this point it is called a foetus. |
| **Implantation** | The process by which the embryo embeds itself into the thick, spongy lining of the uterus. |
| **Placenta** | A temporary organ that develops during pregnancy. It enables exchange of substances (oxygen, nutrients, waste) between the blood of the mother and the blood of the foetus. The two blood supplies do not mix. |
| **Umbilical cord** | The cord that physically connects the foetus to the placenta; contains blood vessels carrying the foetus's blood to and from the placenta. |
| **Amniotic sac** (amnion) | A tough, fluid‑filled membrane that surrounds and protects the developing embryo/foetus. |
| **Amniotic fluid** | Fluid within the amniotic sac that cushions the foetus, allows movement, maintains temperature, and protects against infection. |
| **Menstruation** | The monthly shedding of the thickened uterine lining through the vagina when fertilisation does not occur (a "period"). |
| **Ovulation** | The release of a mature egg from the ovary (around day 14 of the menstrual cycle). |
| **FSH** (Follicle‑Stimulating Hormone) | A hormone produced by the pituitary gland that stimulates egg follicle development and oestrogen production in the ovary. |
| **LH** (Luteinising Hormone) | A hormone produced by the pituitary gland that triggers ovulation and stimulates the corpus luteum to produce progesterone. |
| **Oestrogen** | A female sex hormone produced by the ovarian follicle. It stimulates thickening of the uterine lining and triggers the LH surge. |
| **Progesterone** | A female sex hormone produced by the corpus luteum (and later by the placenta during pregnancy). It maintains the thick uterine lining. |
| **Testosterone** | The main male sex hormone, produced by the testes. Controls the development of male secondary sexual characteristics. |
| **Secondary sexual characteristics** | Physical changes that occur during puberty, not directly involved in reproduction (e.g. voice deepening, body hair, breast development). |
| **STI** (Sexually Transmitted Infection) | An infection caused by a pathogen that is transmitted from an infected person to an uninfected person through sexual contact. |
| **HIV** (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) | A virus that infects and destroys helper T‑lymphocytes, causing progressive weakening of the immune system. HIV is an STI that can lead to AIDS. |
| **AIDS** (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome) | The advanced stage of HIV infection, when the immune system has been severely damaged and the body cannot fight off opportunistic infections. |

## Part 4: Common Exam Errors and Corrections

| Common Error | Correct Answer |
| :--- | :--- |
| Saying fertilisation occurs in the uterus | Fertilisation occurs in the **oviduct** (Fallopian tube) [reference:96] |
| Confusing placenta and umbilical cord | **Placenta** = the exchange organ (nutrients, gases, waste). **Umbilical cord** = the connecting cable. The placenta allows exchange; the umbilical cord carries the foetus's blood to and from it [reference:97] |
| Saying the mother's and foetus's blood mix | The blood supplies **do not mix** – they are separated by a thin membrane. Exchange occurs by diffusion across this barrier [reference:98] |
| Confusing pollination and fertilisation in plants | **Pollination** = transfer of pollen from anther to stigma. **Fertilisation** = fusion of pollen nucleus with egg nucleus inside ovule. Pollination must happen first |
| Writing "the flower becomes the fruit" | The **ovary** develops into the fruit. The **ovule** develops into the seed. Other flower parts wither and fall off |
| Confusing FSH and LH roles | **FSH** = stimulates follicle development + oestrogen production. **LH** = triggers ovulation + stimulates progesterone production |
| Saying progesterone builds the uterine lining | **Oestrogen** builds the lining (Days 6–13). **Progesterone** maintains the lining (Days 14–28) after ovulation |
| Saying "the sperm swims to the ovary" | The sperm swims to the **oviduct** (not the ovary) – the egg is in the oviduct when fertilisation occurs |
| Confusing self‑pollination and cross‑pollination | **Self‑pollination**: same flower or same plant. **Cross‑pollination**: different plant of the same species |
| Saying seeds need light to germinate | Seeds need **water, oxygen and a suitable temperature**. Light is generally NOT required for germination |
| Confusing testosterone and oestrogen effects | **Testosterone** = male hormone (testes, sperm, voice, hair, muscles). **Oestrogen** = female hormone (ovaries, breasts, periods, hips) |
| Saying HIV is the same as AIDS | **HIV** is the virus (cause). **AIDS** is the syndrome (the late‑stage condition of a severely weakened immune system). Not everyone with HIV has AIDS |
| Saying STIs can only be prevented by condoms | Condoms are very effective, but limiting sexual partners, regular testing, education, and antiretroviral therapy are also important methods |
