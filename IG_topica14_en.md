## Part 2: Detailed Knowledge

### 14.1 Coordination and Response

#### 14.1.1 The Mammalian Nervous System

The nervous system is the body's fast communication system. It detects stimuli (changes in the internal or external environment), processes information, and coordinates responses through electrical impulses.

**Central Nervous System (CNS)**:
- Consists of the **brain** and **spinal cord**.
- The brain acts as the control centre: it processes information, stores memories, makes decisions, and coordinates complex responses.
- The spinal cord connects the brain to the rest of the body and coordinates **reflex actions** (rapid, automatic responses).
- The CNS is protected by the skull (brain) and the vertebrae (spinal cord).

**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**:
- Consists of all the **nerves outside the brain and spinal cord**.
- These nerves carry electrical impulses to and from the CNS.
- **Sensory neurones** carry impulses from receptors to the CNS.
- **Motor neurones** carry impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands).

The role of the nervous system is the **coordination and regulation of body functions**. It ensures that different organs and systems work together in a controlled and balanced way.

#### 14.1.2 Neurones and Electrical Impulses

- **Electrical impulses** travel along **neurones** (nerve cells) to transmit information rapidly around the body.
- Neurones are specialised cells adapted to carry nerve impulses quickly and precisely.
- Impulses travel in one direction: from **dendrites → cell body → axon → axon terminals**.

**Three Types of Neurones**:

| Neurone Type | Function | Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sensory neurone** | Carries impulses from a **receptor** (e.g. in skin, eye) **to the CNS** | Long dendron, cell body located outside the CNS |
| **Relay neurone** | Found **inside the CNS**; connects sensory neurone to motor neurone | Short axon, many dendrites |
| **Motor neurone** | Carries impulses **from the CNS to an effector** (muscle or gland) | Long axon, cell body inside the CNS |

#### 14.1.3 The Reflex Arc

A **reflex action** is a means of **automatically and rapidly** integrating and coordinating stimuli with the responses of effectors (muscles and glands). Reflexes are involuntary, fast, and protective.

A **simple reflex arc** consists of five components in this exact sequence:

1. **Receptor** — detects the stimulus (e.g. temperature receptors in skin detect heat from a hot object)
2. **Sensory neurone** — carries the impulse from the receptor to the CNS (spinal cord)
3. **Relay neurone** — inside the spinal cord; passes the impulse from the sensory neurone to the motor neurone
4. **Motor neurone** — carries the impulse from the spinal cord to the effector
5. **Effector** — a muscle or gland that brings about the response (e.g. biceps muscle contracts to pull hand away)

**Why are reflex arcs important?** Reflexes are fast because the pathway passes through the spinal cord rather than going all the way to the brain — this saves vital time in dangerous situations. The brain is informed afterwards.

#### 14.1.4 The Synapse (Supplement)

A **synapse** is a junction between two neurones. It is the point where one neurone communicates with the next.

**Structure of a synapse**:
- The end of the first neurone (presynaptic neurone) contains **vesicles** filled with **neurotransmitter molecules** (chemical messengers).
- There is a narrow gap called the **synaptic gap** (or synaptic cleft) between the two neurones.
- The membrane of the second neurone (postsynaptic neurone) contains **receptor proteins** that are complementary in shape to the neurotransmitter.

**Events at a synapse — the complete sequence:**
1. An electrical **impulse** arrives at the end of the presynaptic neurone.
2. The impulse **stimulates the release of neurotransmitter molecules** from **vesicles** into the synaptic gap (vesicles fuse with the cell membrane and release their contents by exocytosis).
3. The neurotransmitter molecules **diffuse across** the narrow synaptic gap.
4. The neurotransmitter molecules **bind with receptor proteins** on the membrane of the postsynaptic neurone.
5. This binding **stimulates a new electrical impulse** in the postsynaptic neurone.
6. The impulse then travels along this second neurone.

**Why synapses are important — one‑way transmission:**
- Synapses ensure that impulses travel in **one direction only**.
- This is because only the presynaptic neurone contains vesicles with neurotransmitter, and only the postsynaptic neurone has the complementary receptor proteins.
- This one‑way transmission is essential for the nervous system to function as an organised, directional communication network.

### 14.2 Sense Organs

#### 14.2.1 Sense Organs and Receptor Cells

**Sense organs** are groups of **receptor cells** that respond to **specific stimuli**.

| Sense Organ | Stimulus Detected | Type of Receptor |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Eye** | Light | Light receptors (photoreceptors) in the retina |
| **Ear** | Sound and balance | Sound receptors |
| **Skin** | Touch, pressure, temperature | Touch and temperature receptors |
| **Nose** | Chemicals in the air (smell) | Olfactory receptors |
| **Tongue** | Chemicals in food (taste) | Taste receptors |

Each receptor cell is specialised to detect one specific type of stimulus. When a receptor detects its stimulus, it generates an electrical impulse that travels along sensory neurones to the CNS.

#### 14.2.2 The Human Eye — Structure and Function

| Structure | Location | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cornea** | Transparent front part of the eye | **Refracts (bends)** incoming light as it enters the eye |
| **Iris** | Coloured ring around the pupil | **Controls how much light enters the pupil** by adjusting pupil size |
| **Pupil** | Central black hole within the iris | The opening through which light passes into the eye |
| **Lens** | Just behind the iris and pupil | **Focuses light onto the retina** by changing shape |
| **Retina** | Lining at the back of the eye | Contains light‑sensitive cells (**rods** and **cones**) that detect light and generate impulses |
| **Optic nerve** | Extends from the back of the eye to the brain | **Carries electrical impulses** from the retina to the brain |
| **Blind spot** | Where the optic nerve leaves the retina | No photoreceptor cells here; no image is detected in this small area |

#### 14.2.3 The Pupil Reflex

The **pupil reflex** is an automatic response that controls the amount of light entering the eye by changing the diameter of the pupil. It protects the retina from damage by bright light and helps the eye see optimally in different lighting conditions.

**The pupil reflex in terms of antagonistic muscle action (Supplement)**:

| Condition | Circular Muscles | Radial Muscles | Pupil Diameter | Light Entering |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Bright light** | **Contract** | Relax | **Constricts** (smaller) | Less light enters — protects retina |
| **Dim light** | Relax | **Contract** | **Dilates** (larger) | More light enters — improves vision |

- The circular and radial muscles in the iris are **antagonistic** — they work in opposite directions to control pupil size.
- When circular muscles contract, they reduce the pupil diameter; when radial muscles contract, they pull the pupil open.

#### 14.2.4 Accommodation (Supplement)

**Accommodation** is the process by which the eye adjusts to focus on objects at different distances. It involves changes in the shape of the **lens**, controlled by the **ciliary muscles** and **suspensory ligaments**.

**Viewing a distant object:**
- **Ciliary muscles relax** (they become wider, increasing the diameter of the ring of ciliary muscle).
- This pulls the **suspensory ligaments tight** (taut).
- The tight suspensory ligaments pull the lens into a **thin (flatter), less convex shape**.
- Light is refracted (bent) less, and the image of the distant object is focused on the retina.

**Viewing a near object:**
- **Ciliary muscles contract** (the ring of muscle becomes smaller in diameter).
- This **releases tension** on the **suspensory ligaments**, which become slack.
- The lens, being elastic, becomes **thicker and more convex (rounded)**.
- Light is refracted more strongly, and the image of the near object is focused on the retina.

#### 14.2.5 Rods and Cones (Supplement)

The retina contains two types of photoreceptor cells: **rods** and **cones**.

| Feature | Rods | Cones |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Function** | Detect **light intensity** (black and white vision); greater sensitivity for **night vision** | Detect **colour**; three types absorb light of different colours (red, green, blue) for **colour vision** |
| **Sensitivity** | **High sensitivity** to dim light | **Lower sensitivity**; require bright light to function |
| **Distribution** | Found **throughout the retina**, absent from the fovea | **Concentrated at the fovea** (the central region of the retina where light is focused for sharpest vision) |

**The Fovea (Supplement)**:
- The fovea is a small depression at the centre of the retina.
- It contains a very **high density of cones** and **no rods**.
- It is the region of the retina where light is focused for the **sharpest, most detailed colour vision**.

### 14.3 Hormones

#### 14.3.1 What is a Hormone?

A **hormone** is a **chemical substance**, produced by an **endocrine gland**, carried by the **blood**, which alters the activity of **one or more specific target organs**.

Key characteristics of hormones:
- They are chemical messengers secreted directly into the bloodstream.
- They travel throughout the body but only affect **specific target organs** that have complementary receptor proteins for that hormone.
- They produce responses that are generally **slower but longer‑lasting** than nervous impulses.
- They help control processes like growth, metabolism, mood, reproduction and homeostasis.

#### 14.3.2 Endocrine Glands and Their Hormones

| Gland | Location | Hormone Secreted | Main Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Adrenal glands** | On top of each kidney | **Adrenaline** | Prepares body for 'fight or flight' response |
| **Pancreas** | Behind the stomach, in the upper abdomen | **Insulin** (and **glucagon** — Supplement) | Lowers blood glucose concentration (insulin); raises blood glucose (glucagon) |
| **Testes** | In the scrotum (male) | **Testosterone** | Controls development of male secondary sexual characteristics (voice deepening, muscle growth, body hair) |
| **Ovaries** | One on each side of the uterus (female) | **Oestrogen** | Controls development of female secondary sexual characteristics and regulates the menstrual cycle |

#### 14.3.3 Adrenaline — The 'Fight or Flight' Hormone

**Core effects of adrenaline**:
- Adrenaline is secreted by the adrenal glands in situations where the body may be in danger or under stress.
- Its effects include:
  1. **Increased breathing rate** — delivers more oxygen to muscle cells for respiration.
  2. **Increased heart rate** — pumps blood (carrying oxygen and glucose) around the body faster.
  3. **Increased pupil diameter** — allows more light into the eye for sharper vision.

**Supplement — additional metabolic effects of adrenaline**:
- **Increases blood glucose concentration** — adrenaline stimulates the liver to break down glycogen into glucose and release it into the blood, providing extra fuel for muscle contraction.
- This prepares the body for intense physical activity.

#### 14.3.4 Comparing Nervous and Hormonal Control

| Feature | Nervous System | Hormonal (Endocrine) System |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Signal type** | Electrical impulses | Chemical substances (hormones) |
| **Speed of action** | **Very fast** (milliseconds) | **Slower** (seconds to hours) |
| **Duration of effect** | **Short‑lived** (stops when impulse stops) | **Longer‑lasting** (hormones remain in blood for longer) |
| **Transmission** | Via neurones | Via the bloodstream |
| **Specificity** | Impulses travel to a specific location | Hormones travel everywhere but only affect target organs with specific receptors |

### 14.4 Homeostasis

#### 14.4.1 What is Homeostasis?

**Homeostasis** is the **maintenance of a constant internal environment** within the body, even when external conditions change.

**Why is homeostasis important?**
- All cells need a stable environment to work efficiently.
- **Enzymes** — which control metabolism — only work optimally at specific conditions (right temperature, right pH).
- Without homeostasis, cells can become damaged or die.

**Key conditions controlled by homeostasis**:

| Controlled Variable | Why It Is Important | How It Is Controlled |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Body temperature** | Enzymes work best at ~37°C | Thermoregulation (sweating, shivering, vasodilation/vasoconstriction) |
| **Blood glucose level** | Supplies energy for respiration; must be kept steady | Controlled by insulin and glucagon |
| **Water content** | Cells need correct water balance for osmosis | Controlled by kidneys (osmoregulation) |

#### 14.4.2 Negative Feedback (Supplement)

Homeostasis is controlled using a **negative feedback** system:
1. A change from the normal level (the **set point**) is detected by **receptors**.
2. Information is sent to a **coordination centre** (such as the brain, spinal cord, or pancreas).
3. The coordination centre sends instructions to **effectors** (muscles or glands).
4. The effectors produce a response that **reverses the original change**, bringing the condition back to the set point.

**Key principle**: When a change happens, the body responds in a way that **counteracts** the change and restores normal conditions. Negative feedback is the fundamental mechanism underlying all homeostatic control.

#### 14.4.3 Control of Blood Glucose Concentration (Supplement)

Blood glucose concentration must be kept constant to ensure a steady supply of glucose for respiration by cells.

**When blood glucose rises (e.g. after eating):**
1. The **pancreas** detects the rise in blood glucose.
2. The pancreas releases **insulin** into the blood.
3. Insulin stimulates the **liver** and **muscle cells** to absorb glucose from the blood.
4. In the liver, excess glucose is converted into **glycogen** (an insoluble storage carbohydrate) and stored.
5. Blood glucose concentration decreases back to normal.

**When blood glucose falls (e.g. during exercise, between meals):**
1. The **pancreas** detects the fall in blood glucose.
2. The pancreas releases **glucagon** into the blood.
3. Glucagon stimulates the **liver** to break down **glycogen** back into **glucose**.
4. The glucose is released into the blood.
5. Blood glucose concentration rises back to normal.

**Insulin and glucagon work as an antagonistic pair — insulin lowers, glucagon raises.**

**Treatment of Type 1 diabetes (Supplement)**:
- Type 1 diabetes is a condition in which the pancreas produces little or no insulin.
- As a result, blood glucose concentration remains too high after meals.
- Treatment involves regular **injections of insulin** (often self‑administered) to lower blood glucose to normal levels.
- Patients must also manage their diet carefully, controlling carbohydrate intake.

#### 14.4.4 Temperature Regulation (Thermoregulation) (Supplement)

Mammals maintain a core body temperature of approximately **37°C**, which is the optimum temperature for the body's enzymes. Temperature regulation involves the **brain** (specifically the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the body's thermostat and contains receptors sensitive to blood temperature) and the **skin**.

**Structures of the skin** (to be identified in diagrams): hairs, hair erector muscles, sweat glands, temperature receptors, sensory neurones, blood vessels (arterioles supplying surface capillaries), and fatty tissue.

**When body temperature rises (too hot):**

| Response | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sweating** | Sweat glands secrete sweat onto the skin surface. As the sweat **evaporates**, it removes heat energy from the skin, cooling the body down. (Evaporation uses the body's heat — latent heat of vaporisation.) |
| **Vasodilation** | **Arterioles** supplying the skin surface capillaries **dilate** (widen), allowing more blood to flow close to the skin surface. Heat from the blood is lost to the surroundings by radiation. |
| **Hairs lie flat** | Hair erector muscles **relax** so hairs lie flat against the skin; this reduces the insulating layer of air trapped against the skin, allowing more heat to escape. |

**When body temperature falls (too cold):**

| Response | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Shivering** | Skeletal muscles contract and relax **rapidly and involuntarily**. These contractions require respiration, which releases heat as a by‑product, warming the body. |
| **Vasoconstriction** | **Arterioles** supplying the skin surface capillaries **constrict** (narrow), diverting blood away from the skin surface. Less heat is lost from the blood to the surroundings. |
| **Hairs stand up (piloerection)** | Hair erector muscles **contract**, pulling hairs upright. This traps a thicker layer of insulating **air** against the skin, reducing heat loss. (In humans this has limited effectiveness but is a vestigial reflex.) |
| **Insulation** | The layer of **fatty tissue** in the hypodermis provides thermal insulation, reducing heat loss from the body core. |

Additional warming mechanisms include increased metabolic rate (stimulated by adrenaline and thyroxine), and in extreme cold, voluntary behaviours such as putting on extra clothing and seeking shelter.

### 14.5 Tropic Responses

#### 14.5.1 What are Tropic Responses?

A **tropism** is a directional growth response of a plant, in which the direction of the stimulus determines the direction of growth. Tropisms are controlled by plant hormones (chemical signals), primarily **auxins**.

#### 14.5.2 Phototropism

**Phototropism** is a growth response in which parts of a plant grow towards or away from the direction of light.

| Plant Part | Response | Type |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Shoots** | Grow **towards light** | **Positive phototropism** |
| **Roots** | Grow **away from light** | **Negative phototropism** |

**Why is phototropism important?** Shoots bend toward light to maximise the light absorbed for **photosynthesis**, enabling the plant to produce more glucose and grow more efficiently.

#### 14.5.3 Gravitropism

**Gravitropism** (also called geotropism) is a growth response in which parts of a plant grow towards or away from gravity.

| Plant Part | Response | Type | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Roots** | Grow **towards gravity** | **Positive gravitropism** | To anchor the plant and absorb water and minerals from the soil |
| **Shoots** | Grow **away from gravity** | **Negative gravitropism** | To reach light for photosynthesis |

#### 14.5.4 The Role of Auxin in Controlling Shoot Growth (Supplement)

**Auxin** is a plant hormone that controls growth by stimulating **cell elongation**. The following points must be remembered:

1. **Auxin is made in the shoot tip** (the apical meristem).
2. **Auxin diffuses** through the plant from the shoot tip down the stem.
3. **Auxin is unequally distributed** in response to light (phototropism) and gravity (gravitropism), moving to the **shaded side** or the **lower side**.
4. **Auxin stimulates cell elongation** — cells on the side with higher auxin concentration elongate (grow longer) more than cells on the side with lower auxin concentration.

**Explaining phototropism of a shoot using auxin:**
- When light shines on one side of a shoot, auxin produced in the tip moves preferentially to the **shaded (darker) side**.
- The higher concentration of auxin on the shaded side causes cells there to **elongate more** than cells on the illuminated side.
- This differential growth causes the shoot to **bend towards the light**.

**Explaining gravitropism of a shoot using auxin:**
- When a shoot is placed horizontally, gravity causes auxin to accumulate on the **lower side** of the shoot.
- The higher auxin concentration on the lower side stimulates faster cell elongation there.
- This causes the shoot to bend **upwards**, away from gravity.

**Note on roots**: In roots, the response to auxin is different — high auxin concentrations **inhibit** cell elongation in root cells. This is why roots bend **towards** gravity (the lower side has high auxin → inhibited growth → the upper side grows faster → root bends downward). The syllabus focuses on shoot responses; root details provide helpful context.

## Part 4: Common Exam Errors and Corrections

| Common Error | Correct Answer |
| :--- | :--- |
| Confusing the pupil and the iris | The **iris** is the coloured ring of muscle; the **pupil** is the central hole that lets light through |
| Writing "the lens moves" during accommodation | The lens **changes shape** (thicker for near, thinner for distant); it does not move |
| Confusing vasodilation and vasoconstriction | **Vasodilation**: arterioles widen → more blood near skin surface → heat lost (when hot). **Vasoconstriction**: arterioles narrow → less blood near skin surface → heat conserved (when cold) |
| Saying "the brain controls all reflexes" | Reflex arcs pass through the **spinal cord** (or brain stem); the brain is only informed afterwards |
| Saying "hormones travel to a specific organ" | Hormones travel **everywhere in the blood** but only affect target organs that have **complementary receptors** |
| Confusing rods and cones | **Rods** = dim light, black/white, throughout retina. **Cones** = bright light, colour, concentrated at fovea |
| Writing "the ciliary muscles contract to focus on distant objects" | The opposite is true: ciliary muscles **relax** for distant objects and **contract** for near objects |
| Confusing auxin effects in shoots and roots | In **shoots**, auxin **stimulates** growth (cells elongate more). In **roots**, auxin **inhibits** growth |
| Saying "insulin raises blood sugar" | **Insulin lowers** blood glucose; **glucagon raises** it |
| Calling a reflex a "voluntary action" | Reflexes are **involuntary** and **automatic** |
