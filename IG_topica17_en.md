## 📘 Topic 17: Inheritance — Detailed Notes

---

### 17.1 Chromosomes, Genes and Proteins

#### 🔹 Basic Concepts (Core)

- **Chromosomes** are made of **DNA**, which carries genetic information in the form of **genes**.
- **Gene**: a **length of DNA** that **codes for a protein**.
- **Allele**: an **alternative form** of a gene (e.g. the gene for flower colour may have a purple allele and a white allele).

#### 🔹 Sex Determination in Humans

- Humans have **23 pairs** of chromosomes, of which **one pair is the sex chromosomes**:
  - **Female: XX**
  - **Male: XY**
- Sex is determined by the **father's sperm** (sperm carries either X or Y; eggs always carry X).
  - X sperm + X egg → Female (XX)
  - Y sperm + X egg → Male (XY)

#### 🔹 Supplement Content

- The **sequence of bases** in a gene determines the **sequence of amino acids**, which determines the specific protein produced.
- **Different amino acid sequences → different shapes of protein molecules → different functions** (e.g. enzymes, membrane carrier proteins, receptors for neurotransmitters).
- **DNA controls cell function** by controlling the production of proteins, including enzymes, membrane carriers, and receptors for neurotransmitters.

#### 🔹 Protein Synthesis (Simplified)

1. The **gene** coding for the protein **remains in the nucleus**.
2. **mRNA (messenger RNA)** is a **copy of the gene**, made in the nucleus.
3. mRNA molecules **move from the nucleus to the cytoplasm**.
4. The mRNA **passes through ribosomes**.
5. The ribosome **assembles amino acids into protein molecules** in the specific order determined by the base sequence of the mRNA.

> ⚠️ Knowledge of the details of transcription or translation is **not required**.

#### 🔹 Gene Expression & Cell Specialisation

- Most body cells contain **the same genes**, but **not all genes are expressed** in every cell.
- A cell only makes **the specific proteins it needs**, which is why cells become specialised (e.g. nerve cells, muscle cells).

#### 🔹 Chromosome Sets

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Haploid nucleus** | A nucleus containing a **single set** of chromosomes |
| **Diploid nucleus** | A nucleus containing **two sets** of chromosomes |
| Human diploid cell | Contains **23 pairs** (46 chromosomes) |

### 17.2 Mitosis

#### 🔹 Definition

- **Mitosis**: a type of **nuclear division** that gives rise to **genetically identical** daughter cells.
- **Exact replication of chromosomes** occurs **before mitosis**.
- During mitosis, the **copies of chromosomes separate**, maintaining the **same chromosome number** in each daughter cell.

#### 🔹 Roles of Mitosis

| Role | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Growth** | Increase in cell number allows the organism to grow |
| **Repair of damaged tissues** | New cells replace damaged or dead cells |
| **Replacement of cells** | e.g. skin cells, blood cells are constantly replaced |
| **Asexual reproduction** | e.g. vegetative propagation in plants, binary fission in bacteria |

#### 🔹 Stem Cells

- **Definition**: **unspecialised cells** that can divide by mitosis.
- They produce daughter cells that can **become specialised for specific functions** (e.g. nerve cells, muscle cells, blood cells).

> ⚠️ Details of the stages of mitosis (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) are **not required**.

---

### 17.3 Meiosis

#### 🔹 Definition

- **Meiosis** is involved in the **production of gametes** (sperm and egg cells).
- It is a **reduction division**:
  - **Chromosome number is halved**: diploid (2n) → haploid (n)
  - Produces **genetically different** cells (increases genetic variation).

#### 🔹 Key Points Summary

| Feature | Meiosis |
|---------|---------|
| Where it occurs | Testes (sperm) and ovaries (egg cells) |
| Chromosome number change | Diploid (46) → Haploid (23) |
| Number of daughter cells | 4 (in animals: 4 sperm OR 1 egg + 3 polar bodies) |
| Genotype of daughter cells | All **genetically different** |
| Significance | Restores chromosome number at fertilisation; increases genetic variation |

> ⚠️ Details of the stages of meiosis are **not required**.

### 17.4 Monohybrid Inheritance

#### 🔹 Key Terminology (Core)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Inheritance** | The **transmission of genetic information** from generation to generation |
| **Genotype** | The **genetic make-up** of an organism, described in terms of the alleles present |
| **Phenotype** | The **observable features** of an organism (e.g. flower colour, blood type, height) |
| **Homozygous** | Having **two identical alleles** of a particular gene (e.g. AA or aa) |
| **Heterozygous** | Having **two different alleles** of a particular gene (e.g. Aa) |
| **Dominant allele** | An allele that is **expressed if present** in the genotype (shown with a capital letter, e.g. A) |
| **Recessive allele** | An allele that is **only expressed when no dominant allele** is present (shown with a lowercase letter, e.g. a) |

#### 🔹 Pure-breeding

- **Two identical homozygous individuals** that breed together will be **pure-breeding** (all offspring show the same trait).
- A **heterozygous individual** will **not be pure-breeding** (offspring will show variation).

#### 🔹 Genetic Diagrams & Predictions

- Use **genetic diagrams** and **Punnett squares** to predict the results of monohybrid crosses.
- Phenotypic ratios you need to be able to calculate:
  - **1 : 1** (e.g. test cross result)
  - **3 : 1** (e.g. two heterozygotes crossed)

**Example: Pea flower colour (A = purple dominant, a = white recessive)**

Parental genotypes: Aa × Aa

```
        A       a
   A ┌──────┬──────┐
     │  AA   │  Aa   │
     │Purple │Purple │
   a ├──────┼──────┤
     │  Aa   │  aa   │
     │Purple │White  │
     └──────┴──────┘
```

Phenotypic ratio: Purple : White = **3 : 1**

#### 🔹 Pedigree Diagrams

- You should be able to **interpret pedigree diagrams** to determine the inheritance pattern of a given characteristic (dominant or recessive).

---

#### 🔹 Supplement Content

##### 1️⃣ Test Cross

- **Purpose**: To determine whether an individual showing a dominant trait is **homozygous dominant (AA)** or **heterozygous (Aa)**.
- **Method**: Cross the individual with a **homozygous recessive (aa)** individual.
- **Results analysis**:
  - If **all offspring show the dominant trait** → parent is **homozygous dominant (AA)**
  - If **offspring show a 1:1 ratio** of dominant to recessive → parent is **heterozygous (Aa)**

```
AA × aa → all Aa (all dominant)
Aa × aa → ½ Aa (dominant) : ½ aa (recessive) = 1:1
```

##### 2️⃣ Codominance

- **Definition**: A situation in which **both alleles in a heterozygous organism contribute to the phenotype**.
- Unlike simple dominance (where the heterozygote only shows the dominant trait), in codominance **both traits are expressed simultaneously**.

**Example: ABO Blood Group System**

| Genotype | Phenotype (Blood Group) |
|----------|------------------------|
| IᴬIᴬ or Iᴬi | Type A |
| IᴮIᴮ or Iᴮi | Type B |
| **IᴬIᴮ** | **Type AB (codominant)** |
| ii | Type O |

> Alleles: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i
> - Iᴬ and Iᴮ are both **dominant** to i
> - Iᴬ and Iᴮ are **codominant** to each other

##### 3️⃣ Sex Linkage

- **Definition**: A characteristic in which the gene responsible is located on a **sex chromosome** (usually the X chromosome).
- **Key feature**: The characteristic is **more common in one sex than the other** (usually more common in males).
- **Why?** Males have only one X chromosome. If that X carries a recessive allele, there is no second X to "mask" it, so the trait is expressed.

**Example: Red-green Colour Blindness**

- The gene is located on the **X chromosome**.
  - Xᴮ = normal vision (dominant)
  - Xᵇ = colour blind (recessive)
- The **Y chromosome does not carry** the corresponding gene.

| Sex | Genotype | Phenotype |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| Female | XᴮXᴮ | Normal |
| Female | XᴮXᵇ | Carrier (normal vision) |
| Female | XᵇXᵇ | Colour blind |
| Male | XᴮY | Normal |
| Male | XᵇY | Colour blind |

> Colour blindness is **much more common in males** than in females. A female needs two copies of the recessive allele to be colour blind, while a male only needs one.

##### 4️⃣ Extended Genetic Diagrams

- Use genetic diagrams and Punnett squares to predict the results of crosses involving **codominance** or **sex linkage**, and calculate phenotypic ratios.
