## 📘 Topic 19: Organisms and Their Environment — Detailed Notes

---

### 19.1 Energy Flow

#### 🔹 Core Concepts

- The **Sun** is the principal source of energy input to biological systems.
- Energy flows through living organisms:
  - **Light energy** comes from the Sun
  - Converted into **chemical energy** by producers through **photosynthesis**
  - Chemical energy is transferred between organisms through **food chains**
  - Energy is eventually **transferred to the environment** as **heat energy** (lost through respiration)

#### 🔹 Characteristics of Energy Flow

- Energy flow is **one-way** (it cannot be recycled)
- Energy **decreases at each trophic level** as you move up a food chain

---

### 19.2 Food Chains and Food Webs

#### 🔹 Basic Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Food chain** | Shows the transfer of energy from one organism to the next, **beginning with a producer** |
| **Food web** | A **network of interconnected food chains** |
| **Trophic level** | The **position of an organism** in a food chain, food web, or ecological pyramid |

#### 🔹 Classification of Organisms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Producer** | An organism that makes its own organic nutrients, usually using **energy from sunlight** through **photosynthesis** |
| **Consumer** | An organism that gets its energy by **feeding on other organisms** |
| **Primary consumer** | An animal that eats producers (plants) |
| **Secondary consumer** | An animal that eats primary consumers |
| **Tertiary consumer** | An animal that eats secondary consumers |
| **Quaternary consumer** | An animal that eats tertiary consumers |
| **Herbivore** | An animal that gets its energy by **eating plants** |
| **Carnivore** | An animal that gets its energy by **eating other animals** |
| **Decomposer** | An organism that gets its energy from **dead or waste organic material** (e.g. bacteria, fungi) |

#### 🔹 Human Impact on Food Chains/Webs

| Human Activity | Impact |
|---------------|--------|
| **Overharvesting** | Overharvesting a food species disrupts the balance of food chains/webs, potentially causing population explosions or extinction of other species |
| **Introducing foreign species** | Foreign species may become new predators or competitors, disrupting the original food web structure, causing native species to decline or go extinct |

#### 🔹 Ecological Pyramids

##### 1️⃣ Pyramid of Numbers

- Shows the **number of organisms** at each trophic level.
- **Disadvantage**: Can sometimes be **inverted** (e.g. one large tree supporting many insects — few producers, many consumers), so it does not accurately represent energy relationships.

##### 2️⃣ Pyramid of Biomass

- Shows the **total dry mass of organisms** at each trophic level.
- **Advantage**: **More accurate** than a pyramid of numbers because biomass reflects the actual amount of organic material present; it is usually not inverted.

##### 3️⃣ Pyramid of Energy — Supplement

- Shows the **energy stored** per unit area per unit time at each trophic level.
- **Advantage**: The **most accurate** representation because:
  - Energy pyramids are **never inverted**
  - They directly show energy flow, unaffected by organism size or number

#### 🔹 Energy Transfer Efficiency (Supplement)

##### Why is the transfer of energy from one trophic level to another often not efficient?

Reasons for energy loss between trophic levels:

| Reason for Energy Loss | Explanation |
|------------------------|-------------|
| **Respiration** | Organisms convert chemical energy into heat energy through respiration, which is released into the environment |
| **Not digested/absorbed** | Some food is egested as faeces, not absorbed by the organism |
| **Movement and growth** | Energy is used for life processes such as movement and maintaining body temperature |
| **Not eaten** | Some organisms die before being eaten by the next trophic level |

##### Why do food chains usually have fewer than five trophic levels?

- Because there is **significant energy loss** between each trophic level (typically only about **10%** of energy is transferred to the next level).
- After 4-5 transfers, the remaining energy is **too little** to support a population at a higher trophic level.

##### Why is it more energy efficient for humans to eat crop plants than to eat livestock?

- If humans **eat crop plants directly** (producers), energy passes through only **1 trophic level**.
- If humans **eat livestock** (primary consumers), energy passes through **2 trophic levels**, with significant energy lost in between.
- Therefore, in terms of energy efficiency, **eating plants is more efficient than eating animals**.

> Example: 1000 kJ of plant energy → cow absorbs only ~100 kJ → human eating beef obtains only ~10 kJ. A human eating the plants directly would obtain ~100 kJ.

### 19.3 Nutrient Cycles

#### 🔹 Carbon Cycle — Core

The carbon cycle describes the flow of carbon between living organisms and the non-living environment. Key processes include:

| Process | Direction | Explanation |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Photosynthesis** | Atmospheric CO₂ → Plants | Plants use CO₂ and light energy to make organic compounds |
| **Respiration** | Organisms → Atmospheric CO₂ | All living organisms release CO₂ through respiration |
| **Feeding** | One organism → Another organism | Carbon is transferred through food chains |
| **Decomposition** | Dead organisms → Atmospheric CO₂ | Decomposers break down organic matter, releasing CO₂ |
| **Formation of fossil fuels** | Organic matter → Fossil fuels | Ancient organisms buried under anaerobic conditions over millions of years form coal, oil, and natural gas |
| **Combustion** | Fossil fuels → Atmospheric CO₂ | Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ |

**Carbon Cycle (Text Diagram):**

```
    Atmospheric CO₂
        ↑      ↓
    Respiration  Photosynthesis
        ↑      ↓
      Organisms (organic compounds)
        ↑      ↓
    Feeding → Death → Decomposition
        ↑
    Fossil fuels → Combustion
```

#### 🔹 Nitrogen Cycle — Supplement

The nitrogen cycle describes the transformation of nitrogen in the environment. Key processes include:

| Process | Explanation | Microorganisms Involved |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Decomposition** | Breakdown of animal and plant proteins → release of **ammonium ions (NH₄⁺)** | Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) |
| **Nitrification** | Ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) → **nitrites (NO₂⁻)** → **nitrates (NO₃⁻)** | Nitrifying bacteria |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | Atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) → **nitrogen compounds** (e.g. ammonia/nitrates) | Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g. Rhizobium) |
| **Nitrogen fixation by lightning** | High energy from lightning causes N₂ to react with O₂, forming nitrogen oxides that dissolve in rain to become nitrates | No microorganisms needed |
| **Absorption** | Plants absorb **nitrate ions (NO₃⁻)** through their roots | — |
| **Protein synthesis** | Plants use nitrates to make **amino acids and proteins** | — |
| **Feeding and digestion** | Animals obtain proteins by eating plants; proteins are digested and used to make their own proteins | — |
| **Deamination** | Animals break down excess amino acids → produces nitrogenous waste (e.g. urea) | — |
| **Denitrification** | Nitrates (NO₃⁻) → nitrogen gas (N₂) released back into the atmosphere | Denitrifying bacteria |

**Summary of Microorganisms' Roles in the Nitrogen Cycle:**

| Role of Microorganisms | Transformation |
|------------------------|---------------|
| **Decomposition** | Organic matter → ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) |
| **Nitrification** | Ammonium ions → nitrites → nitrates |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | Nitrogen gas (N₂) → nitrogen compounds |
| **Denitrification** | Nitrates → nitrogen gas (N₂) |

> ⚠️ Specific names of individual bacteria (e.g. Rhizobium) are **not required**.

### 19.4 Populations

#### 🔹 Basic Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Population** | A group of organisms of **one species**, living in the **same area**, at the **same time** |
| **Community** | **All of the populations of different species** in an ecosystem |
| **Ecosystem** | A unit containing the **community of organisms** and their **environment**, interacting together |

#### 🔹 Factors Affecting Population Growth Rate

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Food supply** | Abundant food → population grows; food shortage → population growth slows or declines |
| **Competition** | Individuals (same or different species) compete for limited resources (food, water, space, mates) |
| **Predation** | Increase in predator numbers → decrease in prey numbers; decrease in prey → predator numbers also decrease |
| **Disease** | Disease outbreaks can cause a sharp decline in population size |

#### 🔹 Sigmoid Growth Curve

When a population grows in an **environment with limited resources**, its growth curve follows an **S-shaped (sigmoid) curve**. It has four phases:

| Phase | Name | Characteristics | Explanation |
|-------|------|----------------|-------------|
| **1** | **Lag phase** | Population growth is slow | Small number of individuals, slow reproduction rate; organisms are adapting to the environment |
| **2** | **Exponential (log) phase** | Population grows **rapidly** | Resources are abundant, no limiting factors; maximum reproduction rate |
| **3** | **Stationary phase** | Population size **remains stable** | **Limiting factors** take effect (food shortage, limited space, waste accumulation, etc.); birth rate ≈ death rate |
| **4** | **Death phase** | Population size **declines** | Resources are exhausted or environment deteriorates; death rate > birth rate |

**Sigmoid Curve (Text Diagram):**

```
Population Size
  ↑
  |          ___________ Stationary phase
  |         /           \
  |        /              Death phase
  |       /   Exponential phase
  |      /
  |_____/  Lag phase
  |
  └────────────────────────→ Time
```

#### 🔹 Limiting Factors — Supplement

- **Limiting factors**: Environmental factors that prevent a population from continuing to grow.
- At the end of the exponential phase, limiting factors begin to take effect, causing growth to slow and eventually reach the stationary phase.
- Common limiting factors include:
  - Limited food supply
  - Insufficient habitat space
  - Waste accumulation (toxic substances)
  - Faster disease transmission
  - Increased predator numbers
  - Increased competition
