## 13.1 Definition and Major Excretory Organs

### Definition of Excretion

- **Excretion**: the removal of **metabolic waste products**, toxic substances and excess substances from the body.  
- **Key point**: Excretory products are produced by cell metabolism, not undigested food residues.

### Difference between Excretion and Egestion

| Term | Definition | Origin of waste |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Excretion** | removal of metabolic waste, toxins and excess substances | by‑products of cellular activity (metabolites) |
| **Egestion** | removal of undigested food residues through the anus | unabsorbed food from the alimentary canal |

> **Trap**: Egestion (defecation) is NOT excretion because faeces have never entered cells and are not metabolic waste.

### Major Excretory Organs

| Organ | Excretory product | System |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lungs** | carbon dioxide (CO₂) | respiratory system |
| **Kidneys** | urea, excess water, excess ions | urinary system |
| **Skin** | water, salts, small amount of urea | integumentary system (via sweat) |

---

## 13.2 Excretion of Carbon Dioxide via the Lungs

- **Source**: a waste product of aerobic respiration.  
- **Transport**: CO₂ diffuses into the blood and is transported mainly as bicarbonate ions to the lungs.  
- **Removal**: In the lungs, CO₂ diffuses into the alveoli and is exhaled.  
- **Importance**: CO₂ dissolves in water to form weak carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which lowers blood pH. Accumulation would interfere with enzyme function and cellular metabolism.

## 13.3 Urinary System

The urinary system filters blood and produces and excretes urine.

### Main Structures and their Functions

| Structure (Chinese) | English | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **肾** | Kidney | pair of bean‑shaped organs, located at the back of the abdominal cavity; filter blood and produce urine. |
| **输尿管** | Ureter | tube that carries urine from the kidney to the bladder. |
| **膀胱** | Bladder | elastic muscular sac that stores urine. |
| **尿道** | Urethra | tube that carries urine from the bladder to the outside of the body. |
| **肾动脉** | Renal artery | carries oxygenated blood from the aorta to the kidney. |
| **肾静脉** | Renal vein | carries filtered blood from the kidney back to the inferior vena cava. |

> **Trap**: Distinguish between the **ureter** (connects kidney to bladder) and **urethra** (carries urine out of the body).

### Internal Structure of the Kidney (Supplement)

- **Cortex**: outer layer, darker in colour, contains glomeruli and renal tubules.
- **Medulla**: inner layer, lighter in colour, contains renal pyramids and tubules.

## 13.4 Basic Structure and Function of the Kidney

### Two Main Functions of the Kidney

1.  Regulate water content of the blood (essential for maintaining blood pressure).
2.  Remove toxic metabolic products (e.g., urea) and excess substances (e.g., salts).

### Nephron

The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney; each kidney contains about one million nephrons. It begins in the cortex (Bowman’s capsule and glomerulus), goes down into the medulla, returns to the cortex, and finally joins a collecting duct.

**Nephron structure (for understanding)**:

Afferent arteriole → glomerulus (capillary knot) → Bowman’s capsule (surrounds glomerulus) → proximal convoluted tubule → loop of Henle (descending and ascending limbs) → distal convoluted tubule → collecting duct.

## 13.5 Urine Formation

Urine formation consists of three core steps: **ultrafiltration**, **selective reabsorption**, and **urine formation**.

#### Step 1: Ultrafiltration

- **Process**: Blood is filtered under high pressure through the glomerulus to form filtrate.  
- **Mechanism**:
  1.  The afferent arteriole (from the renal artery) branches into a capillary network – the **glomerulus**. The glomerulus is surrounded by **Bowman’s capsule** (like a cup wrapped around the capillary knot).
  2.  The efferent arteriole is narrower than the afferent arteriole, creating high pressure inside the glomerular capillaries.
  3.  High pressure forces small molecules such as glucose, water, urea and ions through the capillary wall and the wall of Bowman’s capsule into the capsular space.
  4.  Blood cells and large proteins remain in the blood because they are too large.
- **Filtrate composition**: glucose, water, urea, ions (no blood cells or large proteins).

#### Step 2: Selective Reabsorption

In selective reabsorption, **all glucose**, **most water** and **some ions** are reabsorbed back into the blood.

- **Glucose**:
  - **Site of reabsorption**: **proximal convoluted tubule (PCT)** only.
  - **Mechanism**: **active transport** – requires energy (ATP) and **carrier proteins**.
  - **Adaptations**: PCT epithelial cells have **microvilli** (increase surface area) and **many mitochondria** (provide ATP for active transport).
  - **Outcome**: Glucose is **completely reabsorbed** in a healthy person.
- **Water**:
  - **Sites of reabsorption**: loop of Henle and collecting duct.
  - **Mechanism**: **osmosis**.
  - **Regulation**: Water reabsorption in the collecting duct is controlled by **antidiuretic hormone (ADH)** to maintain water balance (supplementary knowledge).
- **Salts (ions)**:
  - **Sites of reabsorption**: various parts of the nephron (including loop of Henle and collecting duct).
  - **Mechanism**: diffusion or active transport.

#### Step 3: Urine Formation

- **Definition**: substances that are not reabsorbed form urine.  
- **Composition**: **urea**, **excess water** and **excess ions**.  
- **Pathway**: Urine drains from the collecting duct into the renal pelvis, travels via the **ureter** to the **bladder** for storage, and is finally expelled through the **urethra**.

### Summary Table of Urine Formation

| Process | Location | Purpose | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ultrafiltration** | Glomerulus (inside Bowman’s capsule) | Form filtrate, remove small waste and useful substances | high‑pressure filtration |
| **Selective reabsorption** | Renal tubules (especially PCT) | Reabsorb useful molecules (e.g., glucose) and most water back into blood | active transport, osmosis, etc. |
| **Urine formation** | Collecting duct | Excrete waste (urea, excess substances), regulate water balance | regulated water reabsorption (ADH) |

## 13.6 Role of the Liver in Excretion

The liver converts excess amino acids into urea and also synthesises proteins. This process is essential for removing nitrogenous waste.

### Deamination

- **Definition**: the process of removing the nitrogen‑containing part (amino group) from an amino acid to form urea.
- **Location**: **liver**.
- **Process**:
  1.  Ingested proteins are digested into amino acids, which are absorbed and transported to the liver via the **hepatic portal vein**.
  2.  Some amino acids are used to synthesise human proteins (growth and repair).
  3.  Excess amino acids that cannot be stored undergo **deamination** in the liver.
  4.  The nitrogen‑containing amino group (–NH₂) is removed and immediately converted into highly toxic **ammonia**, which is rapidly converted into less toxic **urea**.
  5.  Urea dissolves in the blood, is carried to the kidneys and excreted in the urine (a small amount is also lost in sweat).
  6.  The remaining nitrogen‑free part (carbon skeleton) can be converted into **glycogen** stored in the liver or used in respiration to provide energy.
- **Importance**:
  - Urea is less toxic than ammonia, but it is still harmful if allowed to accumulate. It must be excreted by the kidneys to maintain homeostasis.

### Assimilation

- **Definition**: the process by which absorbed nutrients are converted into complex molecules needed by body cells.
- **Role of the liver in assimilation**:
  - Using absorbed amino acids to synthesise body proteins (e.g., plasma proteins, fibrinogen).
  - Storing glycogen derived from the carbon skeletons of amino acids.

## 13.7 Importance of Excretion (high‑frequency exam topic)

- **Removal of toxic metabolic waste**: accumulation of urea, CO₂ etc. disrupts normal cell function.
- **Maintaining homeostasis**: regulates body water, salt and pH balance.
- **Preventing osmotic imbalance**: excretion of excess water and ions maintains normal cell osmotic pressure.
- **Removing CO₂**: maintains blood pH stability (CO₂ dissolves to form carbonic acid, affecting pH).

## 📌 Common Exam Traps (Paper 2)

| Wrong idea | Correct statement | Sub‑topic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Defecation is excretion | Egestion removes undigested food that never entered cells → not excretion | 13.1 |
| Lungs excrete water | Lungs mainly excrete CO₂; most water excretion occurs via the urinary system | 13.1 |
| Urea is produced in the kidneys | Urea is produced in the **liver** by deamination | 13.6 |
| Confusing ureter and urethra | Ureter connects kidney to bladder; urethra connects bladder to the outside | 13.3 |
| Filtrate contains proteins and blood cells | Filtrate (in Bowman’s capsule) contains no blood cells or large proteins | 13.5 |
| Glucose is reabsorbed in the collecting duct | Glucose is reabsorbed only in the **proximal convoluted tubule** by active transport | 13.5 |
| All water is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule | Most water is reabsorbed in PCT and loop of Henle; final regulation occurs in collecting duct (ADH) | 13.5 |
| Kidney only excretes urea | Kidney also excretes excess water, excess ions and regulates blood pH | 13.1/13.5 |
