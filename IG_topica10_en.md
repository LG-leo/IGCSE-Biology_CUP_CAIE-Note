## Part 2: Detailed Knowledge

### 10.1 Diseases and Immunity

#### 10.1.1 Pathogens and Transmissible Diseases

- A **pathogen** is a disease‑causing organism. The four types of pathogens you need to know are **viruses**, **bacteria**, **fungi**, and **protoctists**. Viruses are not considered living organisms because they cannot carry out all seven life processes independently — they must invade a host cell to reproduce.
- A **transmissible disease** is a disease in which the pathogen can be passed from one host to another.

**Modes of transmission:**
- **Direct contact**: including through blood and other body fluids (e.g. HIV, hepatitis B)
- **Indirect transmission**: including from contaminated surfaces, food, animals, and air (e.g. cholera in contaminated water, influenza and tuberculosis via airborne droplets, malaria via mosquito vectors)

#### 10.1.2 Controlling the Spread of Disease

| Method | How it controls disease spread |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Clean water supply** | Prevents the transmission of water‑borne diseases (e.g. cholera, typhoid) by removing pathogenic microorganisms from drinking water. This interrupts the faecal‑oral route of infection. |
| **Hygienic food preparation** | Stops food‑borne infections (e.g. *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*) by ensuring food is cooked thoroughly and stored correctly, killing pathogens before they enter the body. |
| **Good personal hygiene** | Hand‑washing removes pathogens from hands before they can be transferred to the mouth, nose or eyes, breaking the chain of transmission. |
| **Waste disposal** | Proper disposal of household and garden waste eliminates breeding sites for vectors (e.g. flies, rats) that carry pathogens. |
| **Sewage treatment** | Treating sewage removes pathogenic microorganisms and nutrients before the treated water is released back into the environment, preventing water contamination. |

All these methods are essential components of public health strategies to reduce the spread of communicable diseases.

#### 10.1.3 Body Defences

**Non‑specific defences (first line):**

| Defence | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Skin** | Acts as a **physical barrier**. The outer layer of dead cells is tough and impermeable, preventing pathogen entry. Cuts and grazes can breach this barrier. |
| **Hairs in the nose** | Trap larger dust particles and pathogens before they can enter the respiratory system. |
| **Mucus** | A sticky substance lining the trachea and bronchi (produced by goblet cells). It traps pathogens and particles. Cilia then waft the mucus upwards to the throat, where it is swallowed or coughed out. |
| **Stomach acid** | Hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach kills most ingested pathogens due to the very low pH (≈2). |
| **White blood cells** | Provide the **second line** of defence. There are two main types: **phagocytes** (carry out phagocytosis — engulfing and digesting pathogens non‑specifically) and **lymphocytes** (produce specific antibodies that bind to antigens on pathogens). |

#### 10.1.4 Antigens, Antibodies, and Specificity (Supplement)

- An **antigen** is a molecule (usually a protein or polysaccharide) on the surface of a pathogen. Each pathogen has its own specific antigens with unique shapes.
- An **antibody** is a protein produced by lymphocytes. Antibodies bind to specific antigens.
- **Specificity**: specific antibodies have **complementary shapes** that fit specific antigens (lock‑and‑key model). When antibodies bind to antigens, they can:
  1. **Directly destroy** the pathogen
  2. **Mark** the pathogen for destruction by phagocytes
  3. **Clump (agglutinate)** pathogens together, making them easier for phagocytes to engulf

#### 10.1.5 Active Immunity (Supplement)

**Active immunity** is defence against a pathogen by **antibody production in the body**.

- It is gained **after an infection** by a pathogen OR **by vaccination**.
- Lymphocytes produce specific antibodies against the pathogen's antigens and also produce **memory cells** that remain in the blood for long periods (often a lifetime).
- On re‑encounter with the same pathogen, memory cells respond **rapidly and strongly**, producing large quantities of antibodies quickly.
- Active immunity is **long‑lasting**.

#### 10.1.6 Vaccination (Supplement)

Vaccination is a method of inducing active immunity without the person suffering from the actual disease.

**The vaccination process:**
1. **Weakened pathogens** or their **antigens** are introduced into the body (via injection or oral drops).
2. The antigens **stimulate an immune response** by lymphocytes.
3. Lymphocytes produce **specific antibodies** that are complementary to the antigens.
4. **Memory cells** are produced that remain in the blood.
5. On future exposure to the actual pathogen, memory cells trigger a **rapid and strong** secondary response — producing large numbers of antibodies before the pathogen can cause illness.

**Role of vaccination in controlling disease spread**: If a large proportion of the population is vaccinated (**herd immunity**), the pathogen cannot easily spread, because there are fewer susceptible hosts. This protects even those who are not vaccinated.

#### 10.1.7 Passive Immunity (Supplement)

**Passive immunity** is a **short‑term** defence against a pathogen by **antibodies acquired from another individual** — the person’s own lymphocytes do not produce the antibodies. The three essential features you must remember are: **short‑term protection, antibodies come from an outside source, and no memory cells are produced.**

**Natural passive immunity:**
- **Across the placenta**: maternal antibodies (mainly IgG) pass into the fetus’s blood during pregnancy, providing protection for the first few months after birth.
- **In breast milk (colostrum)**: the first milk produced after birth contains high concentrations of antibodies (mainly IgA), which protect the infant’s gut from pathogens.

**Key features of passive immunity:**
- Provides **immediate** but **temporary** protection (antibodies are eventually broken down and not replaced).
- **Memory cells are NOT produced** — therefore, once the antibodies are broken down, the individual is no longer immune.
- If the individual encounters the same pathogen again, they will not have any memory cells to mount a quick response and will become ill.

**Importance of breastfeeding**: Breastfeeding provides essential **passive immunity** to the infant at a time when the infant’s own immune system is not yet fully developed. The antibodies in breast milk protect against gastrointestinal and respiratory infections during the vulnerable early months of life.

#### 10.1.8 Active vs. Passive Immunity Comparison

| Feature | Active Immunity | Passive Immunity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **How acquired** | Natural infection or vaccination | Antibodies from another source (mother, injection) |
| **Antibodies produced by** | The individual’s own lymphocytes | Another individual’s lymphocytes |
| **Speed of protection** | Slower (takes days to develop) | Immediate |
| **Duration** | Long‑term (often lifelong) | Short‑term (weeks to months) |
| **Memory cells produced?** | Yes | No |
| **Examples** | Recovery from measles; MMR vaccine | Maternal antibodies via placenta/breast milk; antivenom injection |

#### 10.1.9 Cholera (Supplement)

Cholera is a disease caused by the bacterium ***Vibrio cholerae***, which is transmitted in **contaminated water** (a classic example of water‑borne disease).

**The mechanism by which the cholera bacterium causes diarrhoea:**
1. Bacteria are **ingested** via contaminated water or food and enter the **small intestine**.
2. They **attach** to the wall of the small intestine.
3. They **produce a toxin**.
4. The toxin **stimulates the cells lining the intestine to secrete chloride ions (Cl⁻)** from inside the cells into the **lumen** of the small intestine.
5. The accumulation of chloride ions **lowers the water potential** in the lumen of the small intestine.
6. Once the water potential in the lumen becomes lower than that of the surrounding cells and blood, water moves **out of the cells and blood into the intestine by osmosis**.
7. This massive osmotic influx of water results in **severe watery diarrhoea**.
8. Consequences: **dehydration**, loss of chloride ions, loss of other essential ions from the blood. If untreated, this can be **fatal**.

**Treatment**: Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) — a solution of clean water with a small amount of salt (to replace lost ions) and sugar (glucose helps absorption of sodium ions and water in the small intestine). In severe cases, intravenous fluids may be required.

## Part 3: Glossary (Key Terms Required by the Syllabus)

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pathogen** | A disease‑causing organism (virus, bacterium, fungus, or protoctist). |
| **Transmissible disease** | A disease in which the pathogen can be passed from one host to another. |
| **Antigen** | A molecule (usually protein or polysaccharide) on the surface of a pathogen that triggers an immune response. Each pathogen has its own specific antigens with unique shapes. |
| **Antibody** | A protein produced by lymphocytes that is complementary in shape to a specific antigen; antibodies bind to antigens and lead to destruction or marking of pathogens. |
| **Phagocytosis** | The process by which phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens non‑specifically. |
| **Lymphocyte** | A type of white blood cell that produces specific antibodies against antigens. |
| **Memory cell** | A long‑lived lymphocyte produced during an immune response that remains in the blood and enables a rapid secondary response upon re‑exposure to the same antigen. |
| **Active immunity** | Defence against a pathogen by antibody production in the body, gained after infection or vaccination; memory cells are produced; long‑lasting. |
| **Passive immunity** | Short‑term defence against a pathogen by antibodies acquired from another individual (e.g. across placenta or in breast milk); no memory cells produced. |
| **Vaccination** | The introduction of weakened pathogens or their antigens into the body to stimulate lymphocyte antibody production and memory cell formation, providing long‑term immunity without causing the disease. |
| **Herd immunity** | The protection of a population from a disease when a sufficiently high proportion of individuals are vaccinated, making it difficult for the pathogen to spread. |
| **Cholera** | A water‑borne disease caused by *Vibrio cholerae* bacterium, transmitted via contaminated water; the bacterium produces a toxin that causes chloride ion secretion into the small intestine, leading to osmotic water loss and severe diarrhoea. |

## Part 4: Exam Tips by Paper

### Paper 2 (Multiple Choice)
- **Pathogen types**: Be able to identify which type of pathogen (virus, bacterium, fungus, protoctist) causes named diseases. Common examples: *Plasmodium* causes malaria (protoctist), *Vibrio cholerae* causes cholera (bacterium), HIV is a virus.
- **Body defences**: Know which defences are non‑specific (skin, mucus, stomach acid, phagocytosis) vs. specific (antibody production by lymphocytes). Questions often test this distinction.
- **Transmission vs. control**: Be able to match a disease to its mode of transmission and select the most appropriate control method.
- **Active vs. passive immunity**: Questions frequently test whether memory cells are produced (active: yes; passive: no) and whether antibodies are made by the individual (active: yes; passive: no). Know that passive immunity is short‑term.
- **Cholera mechanism**: Understand that the toxin causes secretion of chloride ions → lowered water potential → water moves out by osmosis → diarrhoea. The keyword “osmosis” is essential for marks.
- **Vaccination question (Paper 2)**: Look for the sequence: a component (weakened pathogen/antigen) triggers antibody production and, critically, memory cells (long‑term protection). Passive immunity involves receiving ready‑made antibodies, not memory cells.

### Paper 4 (Structured Questions)
- **Outline the process of vaccination (5–6 marks)**: State (1) weakened pathogens or antigens are introduced; (2) antigens stimulate lymphocytes; (3) lymphocytes produce specific complementary antibodies; (4) memory cells are produced; (5) on re‑exposure, memory cells trigger rapid, strong secondary response. Use the phrase “antigen‑specific” and “complementary shape”.
- **Explain the cholera toxin mechanism (5–6 marks)**: Step‑by‑step: (1) bacteria attach to intestine wall and produce toxin; (2) toxin stimulates cells to secrete Cl⁻ into lumen; (3) Cl⁻ lowers water potential in lumen; (4) water potential in lumen now lower than in cells/blood; (5) water moves by osmosis into the intestine; (6) results in watery diarrhoea, dehydration, ion loss. Every step must be logically connected.
- **Compare active and passive immunity (4–6 marks)**: Cover: (1) source of antibodies (self‑produced vs. from another); (2) speed of protection (slower vs. immediate); (3) duration (long‑term vs. short‑term); (4) memory cells (produced vs. not produced); (5) give examples for each.
- **Explain the importance of hygiene/clean water/etc. in disease control**: Link each method to its mechanism (e.g. sewage treatment removes pathogens from water before it re‑enters the environment, preventing transmission of water‑borne diseases like cholera).
- **Data analysis**: You may be given a graph showing primary vs. secondary immune response (antibody concentration over time after first and second exposure). The secondary response is faster, larger, and longer‑lasting — because memory cells are already present from the first exposure.
- **Labelling diagrams**: You may be asked to identify a phagocyte on a diagram, or identify a lymphocyte producing antibodies, or a memory cell, or antibodies binding to antigens on a pathogen.
