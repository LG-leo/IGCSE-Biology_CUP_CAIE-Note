## 9.1 Circulatory System Overview

### 9.1.1 Definition and Functions

**Definition**: The circulatory system consists of the heart, blood vessels and blood. It uses the **heart as a pump** to drive blood around the body, and valves ensure one‑way flow.

**Core function of the heart**: As a muscular pump, it contracts rhythmically to eject blood into arteries, driving blood through all vessels and back to the heart.

**Main functions of the circulatory system**:
- Transport oxygen, nutrients and hormones to tissues
- Remove carbon dioxide, urea and other metabolic wastes
- Distribute heat and maintain body temperature
- Transport white blood cells and antibodies for immune defence

### 9.1.2 Single vs Double Circulation

#### Single circulation in fish
- Heart has **two chambers**: 1 atrium + 1 ventricle
- Blood passes through the heart **only once** per complete circuit
- Path: ventricle → gills → body → atrium → ventricle
- Blood pressure drops after passing through gill capillaries, leading to slower flow

#### Reptilian heart (three‑chambered – Supplement)
- Heart has **three chambers**: 2 atria + 1 ventricle
- The ventricle is partially divided, but some mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood still occurs
- Transitional type between fish (two chambers) and mammals (four chambers)

#### Double circulation in mammals
- Heart has **four chambers**: 2 atria + 2 ventricles
- Blood passes through the heart **twice** per complete circuit
- **Pulmonary circulation**: right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lung capillaries → pulmonary veins → left atrium
- **Systemic circulation**: left ventricle → aorta → body capillaries → vena cava → right atrium
- Oxygenated and deoxygenated blood are completely separated

#### Advantages of double circulation (high‑frequency exam topic)

| Advantage | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Higher systemic blood pressure | Left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood to the body at high pressure, ensuring good tissue perfusion |
| Faster blood flow | Higher pressure allows blood to reach distant tissues more quickly |
| More efficient oxygen delivery | Complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood maintains a high oxygen partial pressure in systemic circulation |
| Supports high metabolic rate | Mammals are endothermic and require a high‑efficiency oxygen supply to maintain body temperature and activity |

> Compared to single circulation in fish, double circulation avoids the large pressure drop after blood passes through the gas exchange surface (gills or lungs), so tissues receive high‑pressure, fast‑flowing blood.

## 9.2 Heart Structure and Function

### 9.2.1 Main Structures of the Heart

| Structure | Location / description | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Right atrium** | upper right part of heart | receives deoxygenated blood from superior and inferior vena cava |
| **Right ventricle** | below right atrium, thinner wall | pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (low pressure) |
| **Left atrium** | upper left part of heart | receives oxygenated blood from pulmonary veins |
| **Left ventricle** | below left atrium, **thickest wall** | pumps oxygenated blood to the body via aorta (high pressure) |
| **Septum** | muscular wall between ventricles | prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood |
| **Atrioventricular valves** | between atria and ventricles | close during ventricular contraction to prevent backflow into atria |
| **Semilunar valves** | between ventricles and arteries | close during ventricular relaxation to prevent backflow from arteries into ventricles |
| **Coronary arteries** | on the surface of the heart | **supply oxygen and nutrients to the heart muscle** (cardiac muscle cannot obtain nutrients directly from blood inside chambers) |
| **Cardiac muscle** | heart wall | contracts rhythmically to generate pumping force |

> The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it must generate high pressure to pump blood to the whole body. The right ventricle has a thinner wall because it only pumps blood to the nearby lungs.

### 9.2.2 Heart Sounds (lub‑dub)

Two heart sounds are produced during each cardiac cycle:

| Sound | Cause | Timing | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **First sound (lub)** | **Closure of atrioventricular valves** (mitral and tricuspid) | Beginning of ventricular systole | Marks the start of ventricular contraction; blood is ejected into arteries |
| **Second sound (dub)** | **Closure of semilunar valves** (aortic and pulmonary) | Beginning of ventricular diastole | Marks the start of ventricular relaxation; ventricles begin to fill with blood |

**Mechanism**: When valves close, the sudden deceleration or change in blood flow direction causes vibrations of the valves and adjacent vessel walls. These vibrations are transmitted to the chest wall and heard with a stethoscope. **Heart murmurs** (abnormal sounds) often indicate valve incompetence (leakage) or stenosis (narrowing).

### 9.2.3 Blood Flow Pathway Through the Heart

**Complete pathway (with oxygen changes)**:

Systemic capillaries (deoxygenated) → venules → superior/inferior vena cava → **right atrium** → tricuspid valve → **right ventricle** → pulmonary valve → pulmonary artery → lung capillaries (release CO₂, bind O₂) → pulmonary veins → **left atrium** → mitral valve → **left ventricle** → aortic valve → aorta → systemic capillaries (oxygenated blood releases O₂ and becomes deoxygenated)

### 9.2.4 Monitoring the Heart
- **ECG (electrocardiogram)**: records electrical activity, detects abnormal rhythms
- **Pulse rate**: felt at wrist (radial artery) or neck (carotid artery)
- **Auscultation**: using a stethoscope to listen to heart sounds and assess valve function

## 9.3 Blood Vessels – Structure and Blood Pressure

| Feature | **Arteries** | **Capillaries** | **Veins** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Wall thickness** | thick | very thin (single layer of endothelium) | thin |
| **Lumen diameter** | small | very narrow (only one red blood cell at a time) | large |
| **Elastic fibres** | abundant | very few | few |
| **Smooth muscle** | abundant | none | little |
| **Valves** | none | none | **present** (key feature) |
| **Blood flow speed** | fast | very slow | slow |
| **Typical blood pressure** | high (aortic systolic ~120 mmHg, diastolic ~80 mmHg) | low (arterial end ~30 mmHg, venous end ~10 mmHg) | very low (~0–10 mmHg) |
| **Function** | carry blood away from heart | site of exchange | return blood to heart |

**Blood pressure changes along the circulation**:
- **Aorta** receives high‑pressure ejection from the left ventricle – highest pressure.
- Pressure gradually decreases as blood flows into smaller arteries and capillaries due to increased resistance.
- **Veins** have very low pressure – blood can flow backwards due to gravity (especially in legs). To assist return, **skeletal muscle pump** (muscle contraction squeezes veins) and **respiratory movements** help, while **venous valves** prevent backflow.

**Major differences between arteries and veins**:
- Arteries have thick walls with abundant elastic fibres and smooth muscle to withstand high pressure and maintain flow; veins have thin walls and large lumens to serve as capacitance vessels.
- Arteries contain no valves (except semilunar valves at heart exits); veins contain valves to prevent backflow under low pressure.

## 9.4 Blood Components

Blood consists of **plasma**, **red blood cells**, **white blood cells (phagocytes and lymphocytes)** and **platelets**.

### 9.4.1 Blood Plasma

**Definition**: The liquid component of blood, making up about **55%** of blood volume (the rest is cells). Plasma is about **90–92% water**, with the remainder solutes.

**Main functions of plasma**:
- Transports blood cells, ions, nutrients, hormones, CO₂, urea and heat
- Maintains osmotic pressure and pH balance
- Distributes body heat

**Substances transported by plasma**:

| Component | Examples | Role |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ions (minerals)** | Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻ | osmotic regulation; nerve conduction; pH balance |
| **Nutrients** | glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, glycerol | energy; synthesis |
| **Gases** | CO₂ (mainly as bicarbonate HCO₃⁻), small amount of dissolved O₂ | respiration |
| **Hormones** | insulin, adrenaline | chemical coordination |
| **Nitrogenous wastes** | urea, uric acid | transported to kidneys for excretion |
| **Plasma proteins** | albumin, globulins, fibrinogen | osmotic pressure; lipid transport; clotting |
| **Heat** | — | body temperature distribution |

### 9.4.2 Blood Serum

**Definition**: After blood clots, **fibrinogen** in plasma is converted to insoluble **fibrin**, forming a clot. The remaining straw‑coloured liquid is **serum**.

- Serum = plasma – clotting factors (mainly fibrinogen)
- Serum contains antibodies, hormones, ions, nutrients etc., but no fibrinogen (it has been consumed)
- Used in diagnostic tests (e.g., serological tests for antibodies)

### 9.4.3 Blood Cells – Comparison

| Component | Appearance | Function | Key adaptations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Red blood cells** | biconcave disc, no nucleus | oxygen transport (contains haemoglobin) | no nucleus leaves more space for haemoglobin |
| **White blood cells (phagocytes)** | irregular shape, large nucleus | **phagocytosis** – engulf and digest pathogens | can change shape; rich in lysosomes |
| **White blood cells (lymphocytes)** | large round nucleus almost filling the cell | produce **specific antibodies** | involved in immune memory |
| **Platelets** | cell fragments, no nucleus | blood clotting (thrombus formation) | formed by fragmentation of megakaryocytes |

### 9.4.4 Blood Clotting Process
1. **Platelets** aggregate at the damaged vessel site and release clotting factors.
2. Soluble **fibrinogen** → insoluble **fibrin** (catalysed by thrombin).
3. Fibrin forms a mesh that traps blood cells and platelets, forming a clot.
4. The clot dries to form a **scab**, preventing further bleeding and pathogen entry.

## 9.5 Major Blood Vessels (syllabus requirement)

| Vessel | Type | Blood type | Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Superior vena cava** | vein | deoxygenated | returns blood from upper body to right atrium |
| **Inferior vena cava** | vein | deoxygenated | returns blood from lower body to right atrium |
| **Pulmonary artery** | artery | **deoxygenated** | carries blood from right ventricle to lungs |
| **Pulmonary vein** | vein | **oxygenated** | returns blood from lungs to left atrium |
| **Aorta** | artery | oxygenated | distributes blood from left ventricle to body |
| **Renal artery** | artery | oxygenated | carries blood from aorta to kidneys |
| **Renal vein** | vein | deoxygenated | returns filtered blood from kidneys to inferior vena cava |
| **Hepatic artery** | artery | oxygenated | supplies oxygenated blood to liver |
| **Hepatic portal vein** | vein | nutrient‑rich, deoxygenated | transports absorbed nutrients from small intestine to liver |
| **Hepatic vein** | vein | deoxygenated | returns blood from liver to inferior vena cava |

> ⚠️ **Common trap**: Arteries and veins are classified by **direction of blood flow relative to the heart** (arteries carry blood away, veins carry blood back), NOT by oxygen content. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood, and the pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood – a frequent Paper 2 trap.

## 9.6 Lymphatic System

### 9.6.1 Formation of Tissue Fluid
- At the arterial end of capillaries, blood pressure forces water, glucose, amino acids, oxygen and ions out through the capillary wall (single layer of endothelium with pores), forming **tissue fluid** that bathes all cells.
- About **90%** of tissue fluid is reabsorbed into capillaries at the venous end.
- The remaining **10%** enters **lymphatic capillaries**, becoming **lymph**.

### 9.6.2 Functions of Lymph
- Returns excess tissue fluid to the bloodstream
- Transports absorbed fats (chyle) from the small intestine
- Filters pathogens in **lymph nodes** and participates in immune defence

## 9.7 Coronary Heart Disease

### 9.7.1 Coronary Artery Disease
- **Coronary arteries** supply oxygen and nutrients to the heart muscle.
- High‑cholesterol diet, smoking etc. cause **fatty plaques** to build up → narrowing of the lumen → reduced blood flow to cardiac muscle → angina (chest pain) → myocardial infarction (heart attack).

### 9.7.2 Risk Factors

| Factor | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| High saturated fat diet | raises blood cholesterol, accelerates plaque formation |
| Smoking | damages arterial endothelium; carbon monoxide reduces oxygen‑carrying capacity of blood |
| Lack of exercise | leads to obesity, hypertension, high cholesterol |
| Genetic predisposition (family history) | increases susceptibility |
| Age and sex | risk increases with age; males are at higher risk earlier |
| Stress | raises blood pressure and increases heart workload |

### 9.7.3 Prevention
- Quit smoking; low‑fat diet; eat more fruits and vegetables; regular exercise

## 📌 Common Exam Traps (Paper 2)

| Wrong idea | Correct statement |
| :--- | :--- |
| All arteries carry oxygenated blood | **Pulmonary artery** carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs |
| All veins carry deoxygenated blood | **Pulmonary veins** carry oxygenated blood to the left atrium |
| All white blood cells produce antibodies | Only **lymphocytes** produce antibodies |
| Left and right ventricles have the same wall thickness | Left ventricle is much thicker |
| Red blood cells have a nucleus | Mature mammalian red blood cells have **no nucleus** |
| Double circulation means the heart pumps twice | Double circulation means blood passes through the heart **twice per circuit** |
| Serum is the same as plasma | Serum is plasma minus fibrinogen (clotting factors) |
| Venous pressure is high | Venous pressure is **very low**; relies on valves and muscle pump |
| Hepatic portal vein carries oxygenated blood | Hepatic portal vein carries nutrient‑rich **deoxygenated** blood from the gut to the liver |
