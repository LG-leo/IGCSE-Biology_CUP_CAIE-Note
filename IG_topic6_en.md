## 1. Overview of Photosynthesis

### 1.1 Definition and Importance
- **Photosynthesis**: the process by which plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose (a carbohydrate) and oxygen.
- **Mode of nutrition**: autotrophic – plants make their own organic food.
- **Word equation**:  
  `carbon dioxide + water --(light, chlorophyll)--> glucose + oxygen`  
- **Balanced chemical equation (Extended)**:  
  `6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂`

### 1.2 Fate of Glucose (5 main uses)
| Product | Use | Explanation |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Starch | energy storage | insoluble, does not affect osmotic balance; stored in chloroplasts or storage organs |
| Cellulose | cell wall | provides mechanical support; maintains cell shape |
| Sucrose | transport in phloem | soluble and chemically stable; suitable for long-distance transport |
| Respiration | release ATP | supplies energy for all life processes |
| Other organics | growth & reproduction | synthesise amino acids (needs nitrates), fats, nectar, etc. |

## 2. Leaf Structure – In‑depth Analysis

The leaf is the main organ of photosynthesis. Its structure is highly specialised.

### 2.1 Leaf Cross‑section (from outside to inside)

| Structure | Location | Cell features | Main function | Relation to oxygen/gas |
|-----------|----------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Cuticle** | outer layer of upper/lower epidermis | transparent waxy layer, no cells | prevents water loss, allows light through | does not affect gas diffusion |
| **Upper epidermis** | below cuticle | single layer of transparent cells, no chloroplasts | protection, reduces light blockage | light passes through to mesophyll |
| **Palisade mesophyll** | below upper epidermis | **cylindrical cells, tightly packed, many chloroplasts** | **main site of photosynthesis, produces oxygen** | oxygen produced here diffuses to air spaces |
| **Spongy mesophyll** | below palisade layer | irregular cells with large **air spaces** | gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ and H₂O out) | oxygen diffuses from palisade cells into air spaces, then out via stomata |
| **Lower epidermis** | bottom layer | has stomata (most plants) | protection, controls gas exchange | oxygen mainly exits through lower surface stomata |
| **Stomata** | in lower epidermis (some plants also on upper) | surrounded by two **guard cells** | CO₂ enters, O₂ and H₂O vapour exit | **passage for oxygen release** |
| **Vein (vascular bundle)** | throughout mesophyll | contains xylem (water) and phloem (sucrose) | transport and support | not directly involved in gas exchange |

### 2.2 Why Palisade Tissue is Essential
- Cylindrical shape allows tight packing → more chloroplasts per unit area.
- Located near the upper surface → receives maximum light → highest photosynthetic rate.
- Oxygen produced here diffuses into the air spaces of the spongy mesophyll.

### 2.3 Role of Air Spaces in Spongy Mesophyll
- Irregular cells create large **intercellular air spaces** – a “gas highway”.
- CO₂ from stomata diffuses rapidly to palisade cells; **oxygen from palisade cells diffuses to air spaces and then out through stomata**.
- Without air spaces, gas diffusion would be extremely slow, limiting photosynthesis.

### 2.4 Why Most Stomata are on the Lower Epidermis
- Upper surface receives direct sunlight; stomata there would cause excessive water loss.
- Lower surface is shaded → reduces transpiration while still allowing CO₂ entry and O₂ exit.
- Floating plants (e.g., water lily) have stomata on upper surface (exposed to air); xerophytes may have sunken stomata.

### 2.5 Complete List of Leaf Adaptations for Photosynthesis (Paper 4)

| Adaptation | How it helps photosynthesis |
|------------|-----------------------------|
| Broad and flat | increases surface area to capture light |
| Thin | short diffusion distance for CO₂ |
| Transparent cuticle | allows light through while reducing water loss |
| Palisade cells: cylindrical & packed | many chloroplasts close to light source |
| Spongy mesophyll air spaces | speeds up gas exchange |
| Vascular bundles (veins) | xylem brings water, phloem removes sucrose |
| Stomata mostly on lower surface | reduces water loss while maintaining gas exchange |

## 3. Mineral Nutrition and Deficiency Symptoms (Extended high frequency)

### 3.1 Nitrate (NO₃⁻)
- **Use**: to make amino acids → proteins → enzymes and structural proteins.
- **Deficiency symptoms**: **stunted growth + older leaves turn yellow**.
- **Causal chain**: lack of nitrate → reduced amino acid synthesis → less protein → cell division and growth inhibited → stunted growth; older leaves yellow because proteins are broken down and moved to new growth.

### 3.2 Magnesium (Mg²⁺)
- **Use**: central component of chlorophyll (chlorophyll contains magnesium).
- **Deficiency symptoms**: **chlorosis (yellowing of leaves), especially older leaves** (magnesium is mobile and moves from old to new leaves).
- **Consequence**: reduced chlorophyll → lower photosynthesis → poor growth.

### 3.3 Answer Templates (Paper 4)
> **Explain why nitrate deficiency causes stunted growth.**  
> Nitrate is needed to make amino acids. Amino acids are linked to form proteins. Proteins are required for cell division and growth. Without nitrate, protein synthesis is reduced, so the plant cannot grow properly → stunted growth.

> **Explain why magnesium deficiency causes yellow leaves.**  
> Magnesium is a central component of chlorophyll. Without magnesium, chlorophyll cannot be synthesised. Leaves turn yellow (chlorosis) because they lack green pigment. This reduces photosynthesis.

## 4. Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis

### 4.1 What is a Limiting Factor?
- **Definition**: a factor that, if increased, directly increases the rate of photosynthesis until another factor becomes limiting. When the graph plateaus, the current factor is no longer limiting – something else is.

### 4.2 Three Major Limiting Factors

| Factor | Mechanism | Graph shape | Exam points |
|--------|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Light intensity** | light provides energy for the light‑dependent reactions | rising then plateau (limited by CO₂ or temperature) | shade vs sun plants; light saturation point |
| **Carbon dioxide concentration** | CO₂ is a substrate for the Calvin cycle (light‑independent reactions) | rising then plateau (limited by other factors) | greenhouses add CO₂ to increase yield |
| **Temperature** | affects enzyme activity (e.g., RuBisCO) | rises to optimum then falls (enzyme denaturation) | optimum ~25‑35°C; above ~45°C enzymes denature |

> Water can become limiting in severe drought (stomata close), but it is not one of the “big three” in IGCSE.

### 4.3 Graph Analysis Template (Paper 4)
- “Between X and Y, the rate increases as temperature increases because higher temperature gives molecules more kinetic energy, increasing collisions between enzymes and substrates.”
- “Beyond Z, the rate decreases because the high temperature denatures the enzyme – the active site changes shape and substrate can no longer bind.”

## 5. Key Experiments – Detailed

### 5.1 Starch Test (Iodine Test) – Proving Photosynthesis Has Occurred

**Principle**: Starch is the storage product of photosynthesis. Detecting starch shows that photosynthesis took place.

**Steps (order matters)**:
1. **Boil leaf in water** (1‑2 min) → kills cells, stops enzyme activity, makes membranes permeable.
2. **Decolourise in ethanol** (water bath) → removes chlorophyll, otherwise green colour hides iodine result.
3. **Rinse in hot water** → softens leaf, makes it easier to handle.
4. **Add iodine solution** → blue‑black colour indicates starch.

**Controls**:
- **Destarching**: place plant in darkness for 48 hours before the experiment to use up any existing starch. Then any positive result must come from new photosynthesis.
- **Partially covered leaf**: cover part of a leaf with aluminium foil – the covered part remains yellow‑brown (no light, no starch), uncovered part turns blue‑black (light, starch formed). This shows light is needed.

### 5.2 Aquatic Plant Bubble Count (Measuring Photosynthesis Rate)

**Material**: pondweed, e.g., *Elodea*.

**Method**: place pondweed under water, count oxygen bubbles released per minute under different conditions.

**Variables**:
- Independent: light intensity, light wavelength, CO₂ concentration (add sodium hydrogencarbonate)
- Controlled: temperature, plant size, same light source distance

**Limitations**: bubble number can be affected by temperature, plant condition; repeat and average.

### 5.3 Hydrogencarbonate Indicator Experiment (Qualitative Gas Exchange)

**Indicator**: bicarbonate indicator (changes colour with CO₂ concentration)

| Condition | CO₂ level | pH | Indicator colour |
|-----------|-----------|-----|------------------|
| Initial (atmospheric equilibrium) | 0.04% | ~8.4 | orange |
| Light (with plant) | decreases (photosynthesis absorbs CO₂) | rises (alkaline) | **purple** |
| Dark (with plant) | increases (respiration releases CO₂) | falls (acidic) | **yellow** |

**Control**: a tube with indicator only (no plant) should remain orange, proving that colour change is caused by the plant.

## 6. Gas Exchange – Day vs Night

| Period | Photosynthesis vs Respiration | Net CO₂ exchange | Net O₂ exchange |
|--------|-------------------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Day | photosynthesis > respiration | absorbs CO₂ | releases O₂ |
| Night | no photosynthesis, only respiration | releases CO₂ | absorbs O₂ |

> **Key point**: photosynthesis requires light, so it only happens during the day. Respiration happens all the time.

## 7. Common Exam Traps & High‑Score Tips

| Trap | Correct understanding |
|------|------------------------|
| Glucose is transported in phloem | Phloem transports **sucrose**, not glucose |
| Only one limiting factor at a time | On a graph plateau, the factor tested is no longer limiting – another factor limits the rate |
| Magnesium deficiency first appears on young leaves | Magnesium is mobile; **older leaves** show symptoms first (yellowing) |
| Starch test can be done directly with iodine | Must decolourise with ethanol first, otherwise chlorophyll masks the colour |
| Plants produce no CO₂ in the dark | Respiration continues in darkness, releasing CO₂ |
| Stomata are mostly on the upper surface | In most dicot plants, stomata are more numerous on the **lower** surface to reduce water loss |
