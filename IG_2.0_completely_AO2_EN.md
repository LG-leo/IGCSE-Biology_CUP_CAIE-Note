## 📘 AO2 (Analysis) — IGCSE Biology Cross-Topic Combinations 1~30

**AO2 (Analysis)** requires you to **connect knowledge from different topics** to analyse complex scenarios. Below is a detailed breakdown of the first 30 cross-topic combinations:

---

### 1️⃣ Topic 7 + 3 + 9 — Absorption of glucose/amino acids from small intestine into blood

| Topics Involved | Key Knowledge Points |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **7 Human Nutrition** | The inner wall of the small intestine has **villi** and **microvilli**, increasing surface area |
| **3 Movement into/out of cells** | Glucose and amino acids enter intestinal epithelial cells via **active transport** or **facilitated diffusion** |
| **9 Transport in animals** | After absorption, they enter **capillaries** and are transported to the liver via the **hepatic portal vein** |

**Analysis Points:**
- Villi provide a **huge surface area**
- Villus walls are **thin** (single layer of epithelial cells), shortening diffusion distance
- Rich **capillary network** maintains concentration gradients
- Active transport requires **energy (ATP)** from mitochondrial respiration

---

### 2️⃣ Topic 7 + 3 + 4 — Dialysis tubing experiment (amylase, Benedict's test)

| Topics Involved | Key Knowledge Points |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **7 Human Nutrition** | Amylase breaks down starch into maltose |
| **3 Movement into/out of cells** | Dialysis tubing is a **partially permeable membrane** — starch cannot pass through, maltose can |
| **4 Biological molecules** | Starch turns iodine blue-black; maltose (a reducing sugar) turns Benedict's reagent brick-red |

**Analysis Points:**
- Dialysis tubing models the small intestine
- Starch (large molecule) stays inside the tubing → iodine test turns blue-black
- Maltose (small molecule) diffuses out → Benedict's test is positive outside

---

### 3️⃣ Topic 7 + 5 + 3 — Enzyme action and product diffusion

| Topics Involved | Key Knowledge Points |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **7 Human Nutrition** | Digestive enzymes break large molecules into smaller ones |
| **5 Enzymes** | Enzymes are biological catalysts; they lower activation energy; enzyme-substrate complex |
| **3 Movement into/out of cells** | Small product molecules **diffuse** away from the enzyme's site of action |

**Analysis Points:**
- Enzymes break down substrates into smaller products
- Products are continuously removed (by diffusion or active transport) → maintains concentration gradient → drives the reaction forward

### 4️⃣ Topic 7 + 3 + 4 + 5 — Comprehensive: digestion, absorption, testing

This is a common comprehensive question in Paper 4.

**Analysis Points:**
- **Digestion**: Amylase (saliva/pancreatic juice) breaks starch → maltose; maltase breaks maltose → glucose
- **Absorption**: Glucose enters the bloodstream through villus epithelial cells (active transport/facilitated diffusion)
- **Testing**: Use iodine to test for starch; use Benedict's to test for reducing sugars
- **Enzymes**: Temperature/pH affects enzyme activity → affects digestion rate

### 5️⃣ Topic 7 + 3 + 9 + 12 — Glucose transported to cells for energy after absorption

| Step | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Glucose is absorbed from the small intestine (active transport) |
| 2 | Glucose enters the **hepatic portal vein** → liver |
| 3 | Transported via the **circulatory system** to all body cells |
| 4 | Cells break down glucose through **aerobic respiration**, releasing energy (ATP) |

**Analysis Points:**
- The ultimate fate of glucose: used for **respiration to release energy**
- Energy is used for: muscle contraction, protein synthesis, cell division, active transport, nerve impulses, maintaining body temperature

---

### 6️⃣ Topic 7 + 4 + 5 — Food tests and enzyme digestion

**Analysis Points:**
- Food tests: starch (iodine), reducing sugars (Benedict's), proteins (biuret), fats (ethanol emulsion)
- Enzymes break down large molecules into smaller ones → test results change
- Example: Starch + amylase → maltose → iodine test changes from blue-black to colourless; Benedict's changes from blue to brick-red

### 7️⃣ Topic 7 + 9 + 13 — Waste handling after absorption (urea, etc.)

| Step | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Amino acids are absorbed from the small intestine |
| 2 | The liver carries out **deamination** → produces **urea** |
| 3 | Urea is transported via **blood** to the **kidneys** |
| 4 | The kidneys filter the blood and excrete urea as **urine** |

### 8️⃣ Topic 9 + 11 — Oxygen from alveoli to blood to tissues

| Step | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | O₂ concentration is high in alveoli → enters capillaries by **diffusion** |
| 2 | O₂ binds to **haemoglobin** in red blood cells |
| 3 | Transported via the **circulatory system** to tissues |
| 4 | In tissues, O₂ is released from haemoglobin and diffuses into cells |

**Analysis Points:**
- Adaptations of alveoli: **large surface area, thin walls, rich blood supply, moist**
- Gas exchange is **passive diffusion**, down a concentration gradient

### 9️⃣ Topic 9 + 11 + 12 — Heart rate, breathing rate, oxygen debt during exercise

| Change | Reason |
|--------|--------|
| **Heart rate increases** | Need to deliver O₂ to muscles faster and remove CO₂ and lactic acid faster |
| **Breathing rate increases** | Need more O₂ for aerobic respiration; need to expel more CO₂ |
| **Oxygen debt** | During vigorous exercise, O₂ supply is insufficient → muscles carry out **anaerobic respiration** → produces **lactic acid** |
| **After exercise** | Continued deep breathing and fast heart rate → provides O₂ to convert lactic acid into CO₂ + H₂O (in the liver) |

### 🔟 Topic 9 + 11 + 12 + 14 — Exercise regulation (adrenaline, CO₂ feedback)

| Regulatory Mechanism | Explanation |
|---------------------|-------------|
| **Adrenaline** | Secreted by adrenal glands → increases heart rate and breathing rate, preparing the body for exercise |
| **CO₂ feedback** | Exercise produces more CO₂ → blood pH decreases → breathing centre in the brain detects this → increases breathing rate and depth |
| **Nervous system** | The autonomic nervous system regulates heart rate and breathing rate |
| **Chemoreceptors** | Detect changes in blood CO₂ and pH |

### 1️⃣1️⃣ Topic 9 + 12 — Blood transport of oxygen and cellular respiration

**Analysis Points:**
- **Haemoglobin** in red blood cells binds with O₂ to form **oxyhaemoglobin**
- O₂ is transported to tissues → used for **aerobic respiration**
- Aerobic respiration: glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy (ATP)
- CO₂ is transported back to the lungs via the blood to be exhaled

### 1️⃣5️⃣ Topic 14 + 14.4 — Temperature regulation (skin vessels, sweating)

| Condition | Response | Effect |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Too hot** | Blood vessels **dilate (vasodilation)**; sweat glands secrete sweat | Increases heat loss; sweat evaporation removes heat |
| **Too cold** | Blood vessels **constrict (vasoconstriction)**; hair erector muscles contract (goosebumps); shivering | Reduces heat loss; shivering generates heat |

**Analysis Points:**
- This is an example of **negative feedback**
- The **thermoregulatory centre** in the hypothalamus detects changes in blood temperature

### 1️⃣6️⃣ Topic 14 + 14.4 + 13 — Water and salt balance (or blood glucose regulation)

**Analysis Points:**
- **Water balance**: ADH (antidiuretic hormone) regulates water reabsorption in the kidneys
- **Salt balance**: The kidneys regulate the reabsorption of ions such as Na⁺ and K⁺
- **Blood glucose regulation**: Insulin lowers blood glucose; glucagon raises blood glucose (negative feedback)

### 1️⃣7️⃣ Topic 14 + 14.4 — Blood glucose regulation (insulin/glucagon)

| Blood Glucose Level | Hormone Secreted | Effect |
|--------------------|-----------------|--------|
| **Too high** | Insulin ↑ | Promotes glucose uptake by cells; liver converts glucose into glycogen for storage |
| **Too low** | Glucagon ↑ | Liver breaks down glycogen into glucose and releases it into the blood |

**Analysis Points:**
- This is a classic example of **negative feedback**
- Insulin and glucagon are secreted by the **islets of Langerhans** in the pancreas

### 1️⃣8️⃣ Topic 14 + 9 — Effect of adrenaline on heart rate and breathing

**Analysis Points:**
- Adrenaline is secreted by the **adrenal glands**
- Effects:
  - **Heart rate increases** — delivers O₂ to muscles faster
  - **Breathing rate increases** — more O₂ enters the blood
  - **Blood glucose rises** — more energy available
  - **Blood is redirected** — increased flow to muscles, decreased flow to the digestive system
- This is part of the **"fight or flight"** response

---

### 1️⃣9️⃣ Topic 14 + 11 — CO₂ concentration change stimulates breathing centre

**Analysis Points:**
- During exercise, cellular respiration increases → blood CO₂ concentration rises
- CO₂ dissolves in water to form **carbonic acid** → blood pH decreases (becomes more acidic)
- **Chemoreceptors** (in the carotid arteries and medulla oblongata) detect the pH change
- Signals are sent to the **respiratory centre** (medulla oblongata)
- The respiratory centre increases **breathing rate and depth**
- More CO₂ is expelled through the lungs → pH returns to normal (negative feedback)

### 2️⃣2️⃣ Topic 6 + 8 — Photosynthates transported from leaves to roots via phloem

**Analysis Points:**
- Leaves produce glucose through **photosynthesis**
- Glucose is converted into **sucrose** for transport
- Sucrose is transported through the **phloem** via **translocation**
- Direction of transport: from **source** (leaf) to **sink** (roots, fruits, growing points)
- Roots need sugars for **respiration** to provide energy

### 2️⃣3️⃣ Topic 6 + 8 + 2 — Mesophyll cells, stomata, vascular bundles

**Analysis Points:**
- **Mesophyll cells**: contain many chloroplasts, carry out photosynthesis
- **Stomata**: allow CO₂ to enter, O₂ and H₂O to exit
- **Vascular bundles**: contain **xylem** (transports water and minerals) and **phloem** (transports organic compounds)
- Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata

---

### 2️⃣4️⃣ Topic 6 + 3 + 8 — Stomatal opening/closing and gas exchange

**Analysis Points:**
- **Stomata open**: guard cells absorb water and become turgid → stomata open → CO₂ enters (for photosynthesis), O₂ and H₂O exit
- **Stomata close**: guard cells lose water and become flaccid → stomata close → reduces water loss (transpiration)
- Gases move in and out of stomata by **diffusion**
- This is key to the **transpiration-photosynthesis balance**

---

### 2️⃣5️⃣ Topic 8 + 3 — Mechanism of water ascent (transpiration pull)

**Analysis Points:**
- **Transpiration**: water evaporates from leaf surfaces through stomata
- Evaporation creates a **transpiration pull**
- Water molecules form a continuous column in xylem vessels due to **cohesion**
- Water is transported upward from roots through the **xylem**
- This is a **passive process** (does not require energy)

---

### 2️⃣6️⃣ Topic 8 + 14.5 — Auxin transport and phototropism

**Analysis Points:**
- **Auxin** is produced at the shoot tip
- Unilateral light → auxin **moves to the shaded side**
- Higher auxin concentration on the shaded side → cells **elongate more**
- This causes the shoot to **bend towards the light (phototropism)**
- Auxin is transported through the **phloem** or between cells

---

### 2️⃣7️⃣ Topic 6 + 14.5 — Effect of light on photosynthesis and phototropism

**Analysis Points:**
- **Photosynthesis**: light is a **limiting factor** — light intensity affects the rate of photosynthesis
- **Phototropism**: the direction of light determines auxin distribution → determines growth direction
- Both involve light, but through different mechanisms:
  - Photosynthesis: light as an **energy source**
  - Phototropism: light as a **signal** (stimulus)

### 2️⃣9️⃣ Topic 17 + 18 — Natural selection, antibiotic resistance

**Analysis Points:**
- **Variation**: Genetic variation exists within bacterial populations — some bacteria are naturally resistant to antibiotics (due to **mutation**)
- **Selection pressure**: Antibiotics are used, killing non-resistant bacteria
- **Natural selection**: Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce
- **Inheritance**: The resistance allele is passed on to offspring
- **Result**: The bacterial population becomes resistant; the antibiotic becomes ineffective

> This is a classic example of **natural selection** and a core concept of Topic 18.

### 3️⃣0️⃣ Topic 17 + 18 + 21 — GM crops, insulin production

**Analysis Points:**
- **Topic 17 (Inheritance)**: Genes are segments of DNA that code for proteins
- **Topic 21 (Genetic modification)**: Inserting a human gene into a bacterial plasmid → bacteria produce a human protein
- **Topic 18 (Selection)**: Selective breeding vs genetic modification — both alter organism characteristics, but genetic modification is more precise and faster

**Insulin Production Process:**
1. Isolate the human insulin gene (using restriction enzymes)
2. Insert it into a bacterial plasmid (using DNA ligase)
3. Introduce the recombinant plasmid into bacteria
4. Bacteria multiply and produce human insulin
5. Purify the insulin for use in treating diabetes

### 4️⃣9️⃣ Topic 10 + 12 — Oxygen debt after exercise — how increased breathing/heart rate removes lactic acid

| Topics Involved | Key Knowledge Points |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **10 Diseases and immunity** | (This combination mainly involves physiological responses, not immunity) |
| **12 Respiration** | Aerobic respiration, anaerobic respiration, oxygen debt, lactic acid |

**Analysis Points:**
- During vigorous exercise, O₂ supply is insufficient → muscles carry out **anaerobic respiration** → produces **lactic acid**
- Lactic acid accumulation causes **muscle fatigue and pain**
- After exercise, the body continues **deep breathing and fast heart rate**
- The extra O₂ is used to:
  - **Convert lactic acid into CO₂ + H₂O** (mainly in the liver)
  - Replenish O₂ stores in muscles and blood
  - Restore ATP and creatine phosphate levels
- This extra O₂ requirement is called the **oxygen debt**
