## Part 2: Detailed Content

### 3.1 Diffusion

#### 3.1.1 Definition and Core Concepts

- **Definition**: Diffusion is the **net movement** of particles (molecules or ions) from a region of **higher concentration** to a region of **lower concentration**, i.e. **down a concentration gradient**, as a result of the **random movement** of the particles.
- **Energy Source**: The energy for diffusion comes from the **kinetic energy** of the particles themselves. It is a **passive process** – no additional energy from the cell is required.
- **Movement across cell membranes**: Some substances (e.g. O₂, CO₂, H₂O) can diffuse directly through the phospholipid bilayer of the cell membrane.

#### 3.1.2 Factors Affecting the Rate of Diffusion

Four main factors influence the rate of diffusion, all of which can be investigated experimentally:

1.  **Concentration gradient**: The **steeper the gradient**, the **faster the rate of diffusion**, because more particles move from the high‑concentration side.
2.  **Temperature**: The **higher the temperature**, the **faster the rate of diffusion**. This is because particles gain more kinetic energy and move faster.
3.  **Surface area**: The **larger the surface area**, the **faster the rate of diffusion**, as more particles can cross at the same time.
4.  **Diffusion distance**: The **shorter the distance**, the **faster the rate of diffusion** – particles have less distance to travel.

#### 3.1.3 Importance in Living Organisms

Diffusion is essential for gas exchange and solute transport:

- **Gas exchange**:
  - **Lungs**: Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli (high concentration) into the blood (low concentration); carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood (high concentration) into the alveoli (low concentration).
  - **Leaves**: During photosynthesis, carbon dioxide diffuses from the atmosphere (high concentration) through the stomata into the leaf (low concentration); oxygen diffuses in the opposite direction.
- **Absorption of digested food**: After digestion, the concentration of small soluble molecules (e.g. glucose, amino acids) in the small intestine is high; they diffuse into the blood.
- **Nerve impulses**: At synapses, neurotransmitters are released and diffuse across the synaptic gap to bind to receptors on the next neurone.

### 3.2 Osmosis

#### 3.2.1 Water as a Solvent

- **Role of water**: Water is the most important **solvent** in living organisms. It is vital for:
  - **Digestion**: Water dissolves food, making it easier for enzymes to break it down.
  - **Excretion**: Water dissolves metabolic waste products (e.g. urea) so they can be removed in urine.
  - **Transport**: Blood plasma (mostly water) transports blood cells, nutrients, hormones and waste products.

#### 3.2.2 Definition and Conditions for Osmosis

- **Definition**: Osmosis is the **net movement of water molecules** from a region of **higher water potential** (a dilute solution) to a region of **lower water potential** (a concentrated solution), through a **partially permeable membrane**.
- **Conditions required**:
  1.  A **partially permeable membrane** that allows small molecules (e.g. water) to pass through but prevents larger molecules (e.g. sucrose) from doing so.
  2.  A difference in **water potential** between the two solutions.
- **Water potential**: Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water molecules to move. **Pure water has the highest water potential**. When a solute is added, the water molecules become attracted to the solute particles, reducing their freedom of movement. Therefore, **the more concentrated the solution, the lower its water potential**. Water always moves from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential.

#### 3.2.3 Investigating Osmosis

- **Using dialysis tubing (Visking tubing)**: This is a partially permeable membrane. When a sucrose solution is placed inside the tubing and pure water outside, water moves into the tubing by osmosis, causing the volume (and mass or liquid level) to increase.
- **Effects on plant tissues in different solutions**:
  - **High water potential (pure water or very dilute solution)**: Water enters the cells. They swell and become **turgid** (firm). The pressure of the cell contents against the cell wall is called **turgor pressure**.
  - **Isotonic solution (same concentration as the cell sap)**: There is no net movement of water; cells are neither turgid nor plasmolysed, and may become **flaccid**.
  - **Low water potential (concentrated solution)**: Water leaves the cells. The cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall – this is **plasmolysis**. The cell becomes flaccid, and the plant wilts.

#### 3.2.4 Osmotic Phenomena in Plant and Animal Cells

##### 1. Support in Plants

- **How osmosis supports plants**:
  - Plant cell sap contains solutes and therefore has a lower water potential than pure water.
  - Water enters the cells by osmosis, causing the vacuole to swell and the cytoplasm to push against the rigid **cell wall**.
  - This outward pressure is called **turgor pressure**, and the cell is described as **turgid**.
- **Structural support**: Many turgid cells packed together provide strong structural support for non‑woody (herbaceous) plants, keeping stems upright and leaves extended.
- **Wilting**: If water is scarce, the external water potential falls. Water leaves the cells, turgor pressure drops, and the plant **wilts**.
- **Plasmolysis**: If water loss is severe, the cell membrane detaches from the cell wall – a condition known as **plasmolysis**. Plasmolysis is usually reversible if water is supplied in time.

##### 2. Effects on Animal Cells (A Critical Comparison)

- **Key difference**: Animal cells **do not have a cell wall**; their cell membrane is the only outer covering.
- **In a solution of high water potential (e.g. pure water)**:
  - Water enters by osmosis, causing the cell to swell.
  - Without a cell wall to resist the pressure, the cell eventually **bursts (lysis)**.
  - This is why intravenous fluids must be **isotonic** with blood plasma (e.g. 0.9% NaCl).
- **In a solution of low water potential (e.g. concentrated salt solution)**:
  - Water leaves the cell by osmosis.
  - The cell shrinks and shrivels – this is called **crenation**.
- **Homeostasis**: Animal cells (including human cells) must be bathed in an **isotonic** solution to maintain their normal shape and function, with no net gain or loss of water.

### 3.3 Active Transport

#### 3.3.1 Definition and Characteristics

- **Definition**: Active transport is the movement of particles through a cell membrane from a region of **lower concentration** to a region of **higher concentration**, i.e. **against a concentration gradient**.
- **Key features**:
  - **Requires energy**: The energy is supplied by **ATP** produced during **respiration**.
  - **Requires carrier proteins**: Specific **carrier proteins** in the cell membrane bind to the molecule or ion to be transported. Using energy from ATP, the carrier protein changes shape and moves the substance across the membrane.

#### 3.3.2 Importance in Living Organisms

- **Uptake of mineral ions by root hairs**: The concentration of nitrate and other mineral ions in the soil is often lower than inside the root hair cells. Active transport is used to absorb these ions **against the concentration gradient**.
- **Reabsorption in the kidney**: In the kidney tubules (nephrons), all glucose and much of the water and useful ions are reabsorbed back into the blood by active transport.
- **Absorption of glucose in the small intestine**: Even when the concentration of glucose in the gut is low, the villi can absorb it into the blood by active transport.

## Part 3: Glossary

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Diffusion** | The net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient. |
| **Concentration gradient** | The difference in concentration of a substance between two regions. |
| **Osmosis** | The net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential through a partially permeable membrane. |
| **Water potential** | A measure of the tendency of water molecules to move; pure water has the highest water potential. |
| **Partially permeable membrane** | A membrane that allows some small molecules to pass through but prevents larger molecules from doing so. |
| **Turgid** | Describes a plant cell that is swollen with water, with the cell membrane pressed against the cell wall. |
| **Flaccid** | Describes a plant cell that has lost some water and is no longer firm. |
| **Plasmolysis** | The separation of the cell membrane from the cell wall when a plant cell loses water in a concentrated solution. |
| **Active transport** | The movement of particles from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration, against a concentration gradient, using energy and carrier proteins. |
| **Carrier protein** | A protein in the cell membrane that binds to a specific substance and changes shape to transport it across the membrane. |

## Part 4: Exam Tips

### Paper 2 (Multiple Choice)
- **Concept discrimination**: Clearly distinguish the three processes by their **energy requirement** (passive vs. active), **direction of movement** (down vs. against gradient), and **substances transported** (water vs. other particles).
- **Experiment scenarios**: In osmosis experiments, remember that the water level rises on the side with the **lower water potential**.
- **Factors**: Know how to apply the four factors affecting diffusion to experimental data.

### Paper 4 (Structured Questions)
- **Definitions**: Always include the key terms required by the syllabus (e.g. “net movement”, “down a concentration gradient”, “partially permeable membrane”, “water potential”).
- **Explanations**:
  - When explaining wilting or turgidity, link **water potential difference → direction of osmosis → cell state (turgor/plasmolysis) → macroscopic effect**.
  - When describing active transport, always state that it requires **energy (ATP)** and **carrier proteins**.
- **Data analysis**: In graph or table questions, first identify the dependent variable (e.g. rate of diffusion), the independent variable (e.g. temperature), and then explain the trend using the relevant theory.
