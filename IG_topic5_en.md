## Part 2: Detailed Knowledge

### 5.1 What Are Enzymes?

#### 5.1.1 Definitions and Core Concepts
- **Catalyst**: A catalyst is any substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction, while the catalyst itself remains **chemically unchanged** at the end of the reaction. It is not used up – it can be reused repeatedly.  (Syllabus Core 1)
- **Enzyme**: An enzyme is a **protein** that acts as a **biological catalyst**, meaning it speeds up reactions inside living organisms. All enzymes are proteins, and their function depends entirely on their three‑dimensional shape.  (Syllabus Core 2)
- **Why are enzymes essential for life?** Without enzymes, the vast majority of metabolic reactions in cells would happen far too slowly to sustain life. The body temperature of organisms (e.g. 37°C for humans) is too low to provide enough activation energy for uncatalyzed reactions. Enzymes lower the activation energy required for a reaction to occur, allowing metabolic processes – such as digestion, respiration, synthesis of new molecules, and breakdown of toxins – to proceed at rates compatible with life.  (Syllabus Core 3)

#### 5.1.2 The Lock‑and‑Key Model
- **What is it?** The lock‑and‑key model is a simple way to explain how enzymes work. Imagine the enzyme as a "lock" and the substrate as a "key".
- **The Active Site**: Every enzyme has a special region called the **active site**. This is a pocket or groove on the enzyme's surface with a very specific three‑dimensional shape. The shape of the active site is **complementary** to the shape of its specific substrate molecule.
- **How it works**:
    1. The substrate molecule moves into the active site and fits snugly because its shape is complementary to the active site.
    2. When the substrate is bound, it forms a temporary structure called the **enzyme‑substrate complex**.  (This is the "key in the lock").
    3. The chemical reaction takes place at the active site. Bonds in the substrate may be broken, or new bonds may be formed between two substrates.
    4. The reaction products are formed. These products have a different shape and no longer fit the active site.
    5. The products are released. The enzyme molecule remains unchanged, and its active site is now free to bind to another substrate molecule.
- **Why is specificity important?** The lock‑and‑key model explains why enzymes are usually **specific** to only one reaction. A different substrate with a different shape would not fit into the active site, so the enzyme would not catalyze its reaction. Just as a specific key only opens a specific lock, a specific enzyme only works on a specific substrate. This is called **enzyme specificity**.  (Syllabus Supplement 7)
- **Key terms to learn**:
    - *Active site* – the specific region on the enzyme where the substrate binds.
    - *Substrate* – the molecule that fits into the active site and undergoes the reaction.
    - *Enzyme‑substrate complex* – the temporary structure formed when substrate and enzyme are bound.
    - *Product* – the molecule(s) released after the reaction.

#### 5.1.3 Why Are Enzymes Made of Protein?
- Enzymes are proteins. A protein is a long chain of amino acids that has been folded into a precise three‑dimensional shape. This shape is held together by many weak bonds (such as hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds and disulfide bridges).
- The active site's exact shape is utterly dependent on the overall 3D shape of the protein. If this shape is altered, the active site loses its precise complementary shape, and the substrate can no longer fit. This is the fundamental reason why enzymes are so sensitive to their environment.

### 5.2 Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

#### 5.2.1 Effect of Temperature
_Temperature is the most frequently examined factor in Paper 2 and Paper 4._

- **As temperature increases (from low to optimum)**:
    - The enzyme and substrate molecules gain more **kinetic energy**. They move faster.
    - This increases the **frequency of collisions** between enzyme and substrate molecules.
    - More of these collisions have sufficient energy to overcome the activation energy barrier, making more **effective collisions**.
    - Therefore, the **rate of reaction increases** as temperature rises toward the optimum. (Syllabus Supplement 8)
- **At the optimum temperature**:
    - The rate of reaction is at its maximum. For many human enzymes, the optimum temperature is around **37°C** (body temperature).
    - At this temperature, the enzyme is working at peak efficiency: maximum successful collisions are happening, but the protein structure is not yet damaged.
- **Above the optimum temperature**:
    - The extra heat provides too much kinetic energy to the atoms that make up the enzyme molecule.
    - These violent vibrations begin to break the **weak bonds** (hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds) that hold the enzyme's precise three‑dimensional shape together.
    - The enzyme's shape is permanently changed – this is called **denaturation**.
    - The active site is no longer complementary to the substrate, so the substrate cannot bind. The **rate of reaction drops sharply** to zero once the enzyme is fully denatured.
    - **Key point**: Denaturation is **irreversible**. Once an enzyme has been denatured by high temperature, cooling it back down will NOT restore its activity. (This is a very common exam trap.)
- **Typical graph shape**: Rate of reaction rises steadily → reaches a sharp peak at the optimum temperature → then falls steeply as denaturation occurs.

#### 5.2.2 Effect of pH
_pH is also a very common examination topic, often compared with temperature._

- Every enzyme has an **optimum pH** at which it works best. Different enzymes have different optimum pH values depending on where in the body they function.
    - **Most human enzymes**: optimum pH 7 (neutral)
    - **Pepsin** (a protease found in the stomach): optimum pH 2 (very acidic; works well in stomach acid)  (This is a named example frequently examined)
    - **Trypsin** (a protease found in the small intestine): optimum pH 8 (slightly alkaline)
    - **Amylase** (found in saliva and the small intestine): optimum pH varies (around 7)
- **How pH affects enzyme activity**:
    - pH is a measure of hydrogen ion (H⁺) concentration. An acidic pH has a high concentration of H⁺ ions; an alkaline pH has a high concentration of OH⁻ ions.
    - These ions can interfere with the **bonds** (particularly hydrogen bonds and ionic bonds) that maintain the enzyme's precise 3D shape. (Syllabus Supplement 9)
    - Moving significantly away from the optimum pH alters the charge distribution across the enzyme molecule, causing the bonds that hold its shape to break.
    - The active site loses its complementary shape (denatures), and the substrate can no longer fit.
- **Graph shape**: The "rate vs. pH" graph is bell‑shaped. The curve rises to a peak at the optimum pH and then falls off on both sides as the enzyme denatures at extremes of acidity or alkalinity. Unlike temperature, where denaturation is only at high temperatures, pH denaturation can occur at both extremes of the scale.

### 5.3 Investigating Enzyme Activity (Practical Considerations)

The syllabus requires you to **"investigate and describe the effect of changes in temperature and pH on enzyme activity"** . (Syllabus Core 5) This means you need to be able to suggest a suitable investigation and explain the variables.

**Typical exam approach**: You are asked to design an experiment using **catalase** or **amylase** as your enzyme. Catalase is extremely common in past papers because the reaction produces oxygen, which can be easily measured as foam height or bubble count.

**Example investigation using catalase (potato)**:
- **Enzyme source**: Potato tissue (contains catalase).  Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) is the substrate.
- **Reaction**: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂.
- **Measuring the rate**: Measure the height of oxygen foam produced in a fixed time using a ruler, or count the number of oxygen bubbles per minute.
- **Independent variable**: The factor you change (e.g., temperature by using water baths at different temperatures, or pH by using different buffer solutions).
- **Dependent variable**: The variable you measure (e.g., foam height, number of bubbles).
- **Control variables**: Variables that must be kept the same to make the investigation a fair test (e.g., volume of hydrogen peroxide, concentration of hydrogen peroxide, size/mass of potato tissue, time for reaction).
- **Key exam point**: Why use identical potato cylinders? To ensure the same amount of catalase is present in each test, so any difference in results is due to the independent variable alone.

| Variable Type | Example | Why It Must Be Controlled |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Independent** | Temperature (e.g. 0°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, 60°C) or pH (e.g. buffer solutions at pH 2, 4, 7, 9, 11) | This is what you deliberately change to observe its effect. |
| **Dependent** | Height of foam (mm) / number of bubbles per minute | This is what you measure; it tells you how active the enzyme was. |
| **Control** | Volume and concentration of hydrogen peroxide; mass/size of potato tissue; time allowed for reaction | Everything else must stay the same so you can be confident that the independent variable alone caused the effect you see. |

- **Important**: Boiled (cooked) potato is sometimes used as a control to show that denatured catalase will not produce any oxygen. This is a common P2/P4 question: "Explain why no foam was produced when cooked potato was used." The answer is that the high temperature during cooking caused the catalase enzyme to denature (the active site shape was permanently destroyed).

### 5.4 Exam Traps and Key Distinctions

- **Trap 1: “Killed” vs. “Denatured”**
    - Enzymes are **denatured**, NOT "killed". They were never alive. (Enzymes are proteins, not organisms).
- **Trap 2: Explaining the temperature graph fully**
    - When asked to explain the shape of a rate‑vs‑temperature graph, you must:
        1. Explain the **rise** (more kinetic energy, more successful collisions).
        2. Identify the **optimum** temperature.
        3. Explain the **fall** (bonds break, active site denatures, substrate no longer fits).
    - If you only describe the graph (“it goes up, then goes down”) without explaining why, you will lose marks.
- **Trap 3: Reversibility**
    - Denaturation by high temperature is **irreversible**. Cooling will not restore activity.
    - pH denaturation at extremes may also be irreversible if the bonds are permanently disrupted.
- **Trap 4: Not all enzymes have optimum pH 7**
    - Pepsin in the stomach has optimum pH around 2. This is a classic exam fact. If a question mentions "stomach" or "gastric juice", think of pepsin and acidic pH.
- **Trap 5: Confusing independent and dependent variables in practical questions**
    - The independent variable is the one you change deliberately (e.g. temperature, pH).
    - The dependent variable is what you measure (e.g. volume of oxygen, time taken for starch to disappear).
- **Trap 6: Saying "rate increases with temperature" without qualification**
    - This is only true up to the optimum temperature. Beyond that, rate falls. Always mention "up to the optimum".

## Part 3: Glossary (Key Terms Required by the Syllabus)

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Catalyst** | A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction and is not changed by the reaction. |
| **Enzyme** | A protein that functions as a biological catalyst, speeding up metabolic reactions in living organisms. |
| **Active site** | The specific region on an enzyme's surface where the substrate binds. Its shape is complementary to that of the substrate. |
| **Substrate** | The molecule on which an enzyme acts. It fits into the enzyme's active site. |
| **Enzyme‑substrate complex** | The temporary structure formed when a substrate molecule binds to the active site of an enzyme. |
| **Product** | The molecule(s) released from the active site after the enzyme‑catalyzed reaction is complete. |
| **Specificity** | The property of an enzyme whereby it catalyses only one type of reaction, due to the complementary shape of its active site. |
| **Denaturation** | The permanent change in the three‑dimensional shape of an enzyme (or other protein), causing the active site to lose its complementary shape, so the substrate can no longer bind. Caused by extremes of temperature or pH. (Irreversible). |
| **Optimum temperature** | The temperature at which an enzyme works at its maximum rate. |
| **Optimum pH** | The pH at which an enzyme works at its maximum rate. (Different enzymes have different optimum pH values). |
| **Kinetic energy** | The energy of motion. Increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of particles, making them move faster and collide more frequently. |

## Part 4: Exam Tips by Paper

### Paper 2 (Multiple Choice)
- **Lock‑and‑key diagrams**: Be prepared to identify the active site, substrate, and enzyme‑substrate complex on a diagram. These questions test your understanding that the shapes are complementary.
- **Graph interpretation**: You may be given a rate‑vs‑temperature or rate‑vs‑pH graph and asked to identify the optimum, or to explain why the curve drops on either side of the optimum. Read axes carefully.
- **Denaturation definition**: Multiple choice questions often test the precise definition of denaturation. The key idea is "permanent change of shape of the active site, so substrate no longer fits".
- **Named enzyme facts**: Know that pepsin works best at pH 2 (stomach). Know that catalase breaks down hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen. These appear frequently.

### Paper 4 (Structured Questions)
- **5‑ to 6‑mark 'explain' questions**: A common Paper 4 question is: "Explain the effect of temperature on enzyme activity." You must provide a full, structured answer:
    1. State that as temperature increases, kinetic energy of molecules increases, causing more frequent successful collisions between enzyme and substrate, so rate increases.
    2. Mention the optimum temperature where rate is highest (you can say "around 37°C for most human enzymes").
    3. Explain that above optimum, the high temperature breaks bonds holding the enzyme's 3D shape, causing denaturation – the active site loses its complementary shape, so substrate no longer binds, and rate falls sharply.
    4. State that denaturation is permanent.
- **Practical-based questions**: You could be asked to plan an investigation into the effect of temperature or pH on enzyme activity. Always specify:
    - The enzyme and substrate you would use (e.g., catalase + hydrogen peroxide).
    - How you will change and measure the independent variable (e.g., water baths at 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, 50°C, 60°C using a thermometer).
    - How you will measure the dependent variable (e.g., height of foam in mm after 2 minutes).
    - The control variables you will keep the same (volume and concentration of hydrogen peroxide, mass of potato, time of reaction).
    - How you will ensure reliability (repeat experiment three times, calculate average).
    - Safety precautions (wear goggles, hydrogen peroxide is an irritant).
- **Expect graph‑drawing**: In Paper 4, you may be asked to plot a graph of enzyme activity vs. a given variable. Always use a sharp pencil, label axes with quantity and units, use appropriate scales, and draw a smooth curve of best fit through the points.
